## Part 1: THE WHY Behind Each Methodology

### Source A: Marathon Preparation Methodology

**THE WHY:**
- The marathon is fundamentally a *fuel management* problem
- Lactate threshold is the #1 limiter for any event >30 minutes
- Therefore, training must prioritize LT development above all
- Long runs aren't about "getting used to distance" - they're about teaching muscles to store glycogen and burn fat
- VO2max intervals exist not for the aerobic benefit alone, but to *raise the ceiling* so a higher percentage of that ceiling can be sustained

**Key Insight:** Every workout targets a specific physiological adaptation. If you can't name the adaptation, you shouldn't do the workout.

---

### Source B: Training Intensity System (E/M/T/I/R)

**THE WHY:**
- Different intensities stress different physiological systems
- The body doesn't know "miles" - it knows *time under stress*
- Easy running develops the heart (stroke volume maxes at ~60% HR)
- Threshold running teaches the body to *clear* lactate, not just tolerate it
- Interval training spends time at VO2max to stimulate adaptation
- Repetition training is about *speed and economy*, not fitness

**Key Insight:** "What is the purpose of this workout?" If you can't answer, don't do it. The question isn't optional - it's the entire philosophy.

---

### Source C: 80/20 Intensity Distribution

**THE WHY:**
- Recreational runners run too hard too often because pushing feels productive
- But chronic moderate intensity creates chronic fatigue
- Elite athletes across ALL endurance sports converge on 80/20 independently
- This isn't coincidence - it's the body's optimal adaptation pattern
- 80% low intensity allows *recovery between quality sessions*
- The 20% high intensity is more effective BECAUSE of the 80% easy

**Key Insight:** The purpose of easy running isn't just recovery - it's creating the CONDITIONS for hard work to be productive.

---

### Source D: Cumulative Fatigue Method

**THE WHY:**
- Race day, you run 26.2 miles on *tired* legs
- Training 20 miles on fresh legs doesn't simulate that
- But training 16 miles after days of consistent running DOES
- The fatigue is the point - it teaches the body to perform while depleted
- This also prevents the "weekend warrior" pattern that leads to injury

**Key Insight:** The long run is not the training - the *accumulated fatigue before the long run* is the training.

---

### Source E: Adaptive Training Philosophy

**THE WHY:**
- Cookie-cutter plans fail because runners are individuals
- The same workout affects different runners differently
- But runners don't naturally observe their own responses
- A coach's job isn't to prescribe workouts - it's to help athletes *know themselves*
- Plans should be guidelines, not mandates
- Response to training matters more than adherence to plan

**Key Insight:** The two biggest mistakes are (1) copying someone else's success formula and (2) training by calendar instead of by body.

---

### Source F: Science-Based Training

**THE WHY:**
- Scientists and coaches have been working in separate silos
- Scientists dismiss what coaches do; coaches ignore research
- But BOTH have pieces of the truth
- VO2max is overrated as a limiter - the brain controls performance
- There are no "magic zones" - adaptation is a spectrum
- Zones are a *communication tool*, not physiological reality

**Key Insight:** "There are no magical training zones that maximize adaptation." The spectrum is continuous; zones are artificial.

---

### Source G: Speed Development (5K Focus)

**THE WHY:**
- The 5K is unique: requires BOTH miler speed AND marathoner endurance
- Pure volume fails; pure intensity fails
- Only multi-faceted approach works
- Easy runs matter even for short races (aerobic base)
- Hill work develops power without injury risk
- Goals must be incremental - unrealistic targets create negative environments

**Key Insight:** "Training for a faster 5K has more to do with variety, fun, and self-confidence than pain, discipline, and sacrifice."

---

### Source H: Holistic + Scientific Approach (Verde TC via [verdetrackclub.com](https://www.verdetrackclub.com/))

**THE WHY:**
- Consistency requires enjoyment
- Training that isn't sustainable isn't effective
- The holistic approach (whole athlete) + scientific backing creates results
- Works for Olympic medalists AND first-time 5K finishers
- The founder's injury history informed a focus on sustainability over heroics

**Key Insight:** Consistency AND enjoyment are non-negotiable. You can't have long-term success with training you dread.

## Part 3: Key Tensions (Where Sources Disagree)

### 1. Long Run Length

| Position | Reasoning |
|----------|-----------|
| Source A: 20-23 miles | Specific preparation for race distance |
| Source D: 16 miles max | Cumulative fatigue does the work; fresh 20 ≠ fatigued 16 |
| Source B: 150 min max | Time matters more than distance |

**StrideIQ Resolution:** Context determines. Cumulative fatigue runners benefit from shorter; fresh-legs runners need longer. Individual data decides.

---

### 2. VO2max Training Value

| Position | Reasoning |
|----------|-----------|
| Source B: Critical | Targets aerobic ceiling, essential for performance |
| Source F: Overrated | Doesn't fully explain performance; brain is the limiter |

**StrideIQ Resolution:** VO2max work has a place in the sharpening phase, but isn't the primary driver for distance runners.

---

### 3. Training Zones

| Position | Reasoning |
|----------|-----------|
| Most sources: Use zones | Practical communication tool |
| Source F: No magic zones | Zones misrepresent continuous physiological reality |

**StrideIQ Resolution:** We use workout TYPES and effort DESCRIPTIONS, not zone numbers. Athletes never see "Zone 3."

## Part 4: The StrideIQ Corrections

Based on Michael's experience + these sources:

### 1. Age is a Variable, Not a Limiter
Population research says older = slower.
Individual data (PRs at 57) proves otherwise.
**Action:** Age informs initial assumptions but individual data overrides immediately.

### 2. Intervals: Timing > Activity
Conventional: "Intervals are dangerous for older runners."
Reality: Intervals in BASE (fresh legs) = safe. Intervals mid-BUILD (cumulative fatigue) = injury.
**Action:** Place speed work early in training cycle, not mid-build.

### 3. Easy Pace Calculators Are Too Aggressive
Standard tables derive easy pace from race performance.
Reality: These paces are often too fast; easy should be *easy*.
**Action:** Our Training Pace Calculator shows ranges; we emphasize the slower end.

### 4. Zones Are a Crutch
All sources use zones because they're convenient.
Reality: Zones are artificial boundaries on a continuous spectrum.
**Action:** We use workout types + effort descriptions, never "Zone X."
