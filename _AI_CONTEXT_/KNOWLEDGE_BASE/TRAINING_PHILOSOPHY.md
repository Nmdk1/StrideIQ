## Part 1: The Five Pillars

### Pillar 1: Easy Must Be Easy

**The Principle:**  
80% of running should feel boring. If you can't hold a conversation, you're going too fast.

**The Why:**  
- Cardiac stroke volume maximizes at ~60% HR — you don't need to push harder for the aerobic benefit
- Hard running requires recovery; easy running IS recovery
- The purpose of easy running is to CREATE CONDITIONS for hard work to be productive
- Most recreational runners run too hard too often because pushing feels productive — but chronic moderate intensity creates chronic fatigue

**The Rule:**  
Easy pace is NOT derived from race performance. It is derived from perceived effort: can you talk in complete sentences? If not, slow down.

---

### Pillar 2: Every Workout Has a Purpose

**The Principle:**  
Before any run, you should be able to answer: "What adaptation am I targeting?"

**The Why:**  
- The body doesn't know "miles" — it knows time under specific stress
- Different intensities stress different physiological systems
- Random stress doesn't produce targeted adaptation
- If you can't name the purpose, you shouldn't do the workout

**The Purposes (in order of frequency):**

| Purpose | Workout Types | % of Training |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Aerobic base, recovery | Easy, Recovery, Long (easy) | 70-80% |
| Lactate threshold | Tempo, Cruise intervals, T-work | 10-15% |
| Race-specific | MP runs, Goal pace, Simulation | 5-10% |
| Speed, economy | Strides, Hills, Reps | 2-5% |
| VO2max (ceiling) | Intervals (sparingly) | 0-5% |

---

### Pillar 3: Consistency Beats Intensity

**The Principle:**  
Six months of steady training beats six weeks of heroic training every time.

**The Why:**  
- Adaptation is cumulative; sporadic efforts don't compound
- Injury ends consistency; intensity causes injury
- The athlete who trains 50 weeks/year beats the one who trains 30 weeks at higher intensity
- Frequency builds durability — the body adapts to regular stimulus

**The Rule:**  
When in doubt, choose the option that keeps you running tomorrow.

**For Mid-Mileage Runners (40-55 mpw):**  
The single biggest factor for success is frequency. Move them to 6 days per week. The goal is mechanical and aerobic durability, adaptation to time on feet, running while fatigued. Before they try aggressive workouts, they must BUILD THEIR BASE to run more and more often.

---

### Pillar 4: Recovery Is Training

**The Principle:**  
Adaptation happens during rest, not during work. Work is the stimulus; rest is when you get faster.

**The Why:**  
- Muscle protein synthesis peaks 24-48 hours after training stimulus
- Sleep is when growth hormone spikes and tissue repairs
- Under-recovery = under-adaptation, even with "perfect" training
- The workout you skip to recover is often more valuable than the workout you force

**The Rule:**  
If choosing between a hard workout on tired legs or an easy day, choose easy. Tired legs don't adapt — they break.

---

### Pillar 5: The Athlete Is The Blueprint

**The Principle:**  
Methodologies are building blocks, not blueprints. The athlete's data, history, and responses define the plan.

**The Why:**  
- Books describe what works in general, not what works for YOU
- The same workout affects different runners differently
- Population research predicts averages; you are not an average
- Age, experience, injury history, life stress — these are variables, not limiters

**The Rule:**  
When book principle conflicts with individual data, individual data wins. Always.

## Part 2: Periodization Philosophy

### The Phases

| Phase | Focus | Duration | Quality Sessions/Week |
|-------|-------|----------|----------------------|
| **Base** | Aerobic foundation, volume building | 3-4 weeks | 0 (easy + strides/hills only) |
| **Build 1** | Threshold introduction | 3 weeks | 1 (T-work) |
| **Build 2** | Marathon pace integration | 3 weeks | 1 (alternating T and MP) |
| **Peak** | Race-specific, hold volume | 3 weeks | 1 (MP focus) |
| **Taper** | Reduce fatigue, maintain fitness | 10-14 days | 1 (short, sharp) |

### The Rhythm

**Cut-back Weeks:**  
- Every 4th week (default)
- Every 3rd week (for those who need more recovery)
- Volume drops 25%
- Intensity drops to maintenance or zero

**Intensity Ceiling:**  
- 5 weeks of combined T + MP work is the ceiling before mandatory cut-back
- Never stack weekly intensity + hard long run (pick one)

### The Weekly Structure

**Option A: Intensity Week + Easy Long**
```
Sunday:    Long run (easy, pure aerobic)
Monday:    Rest / Gym / Cross-train (no running)
Tuesday:   Medium-long easy OR easy
Wednesday: Easy
Thursday:  QUALITY (T-work or hills)
Friday:    Easy
Saturday:  Easy (pre-long run)
```

**Option B: Easy Week + Workout Long**
```
Sunday:    Long run WITH workout (MP blocks, fast finish, etc.)
Monday:    Rest / Gym
Tuesday:   Easy (recovery from LR)
Wednesday: Easy
Thursday:  Easy + strides (light)
Friday:    Easy
Saturday:  Easy
```

**The Rule:**  
If there are two intense sessions during the week, the long run is easy.  
If only one intense session (e.g., 15 miles with 6 at MP), the long run can also have a workout.  
Never three quality days.

## Part 3: Workout Definitions

### Easy Running (E)
- **Feel:** Conversational, can speak in complete sentences
- **HR:** <75% max
- **Purpose:** Aerobic base, recovery, adaptation
- **Frequency:** 70-80% of all running

### Strides
- **Structure:** 4-8 × 15-30 seconds, fast but relaxed (NOT sprinting)
- **Recovery:** Full (walk back)
- **Purpose:** Neuromuscular activation, running economy
- **When:** End of easy runs, 2-3x per week in base phase
- **Note:** This is the safest speed work — low injury risk, high benefit

### Hill Sprints (Short)
- **Structure:** 6-12 × 8-12 seconds MAX effort uphill
- **Grade:** 6-10% incline
- **Recovery:** Full (2-3 min walk down)
- **Purpose:** Neuromuscular power, economy, force production
- **When:** End of easy runs in base phase
- **Note:** For older runners, this is safer than flat intervals

### Threshold (T)
- **Feel:** "Comfortably hard" — can speak in short phrases, not sentences
- **HR:** 83-90% max
- **Pace:** ~10-15 sec/mi slower than 10K race pace
- **Duration:** 20-40 min continuous OR intervals with short rest (60-90s)
- **Purpose:** Raise lactate threshold — the #1 limiter for any event >30 minutes
- **Limit:** Max 10% of weekly mileage

**T-Block Progression (Example):**
```
Week 1: 6 × 5 min @ T, 2 min jog
Week 2: 5 × 6 min @ T, 2 min jog
Week 3: 4 × 8 min @ T, 2 min jog
Week 4: 3 × 10 min @ T, 3 min jog
Week 5: 2 × 15 min @ T, 3 min jog
Week 6: 30-40 min continuous @ T
```

**Note:** Aggressive T-block progressions are FINE. T-work poses the least injury risk and maximum benefit. Everyone serious about racing a marathon can handle threshold.

### Marathon Pace (MP)
- **Pace:** Goal marathon race pace exactly
- **Purpose:** Race-specific fitness, fueling practice, confidence
- **Progression:** Start at 4 miles, build to 8-10 miles
- **Placement:** Inside long runs (never standalone 2 days before long run)

**MP Long Run Examples:**
```
Base:  20 miles easy (no MP)
Build: 18 miles with last 4 @ MP
Peak:  20 miles with 6-8 @ MP (mid-run or fast finish)
Dress Rehearsal: 20 miles with 8 @ MP, race nutrition, race gear
```

### Long Run
- **Duration:** 14-24 miles depending on phase
- **Pace:** 10-20% slower than marathon pace (when easy)
- **Purpose:** Endurance, fat oxidation, mental toughness, glycogen storage
- **Day:** Sunday (allows Monday rest, Thursday quality, full recovery cycle)
- **Progression:** Build from 14 → 22-24 miles over training cycle
- **Peak:** Hold within 2-4 miles of peak for 3 weeks before taper

### Medium-Long Run
- **Distance:** 70-75% of long run distance (10-15 miles for marathon)
- **Purpose:** Build endurance without full long-run recovery cost
- **When:** Mid-week (Tuesday) in build/peak phases
- **Note:** For mid-mileage runners, this may just be a longer easy run (8-11 miles)

### Intervals (I)
- **Structure:** 3-5 min repeats at 5K pace
- **Recovery:** Equal to or slightly less than work time
- **Purpose:** Raise VO2max ceiling
- **Limit:** Max 8% of weekly mileage

**CRITICAL — Timing Is Everything:**

| Phase | Volume | Cumulative Fatigue | Intervals | Result |
|-------|--------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| Base/Pre-build | Moderate | Low | Safe, productive | Speed development ✓ |
| Mid-build | High (65+ mpw) | HIGH | Injury risk | Avoid ✗ |

Intervals are NOT inherently dangerous. The injury risk comes from WHEN they're introduced.
- In base phase (fresh legs, low fatigue): Safe and effective
- In mid-build (high volume, MP work stacking): High injury risk
- Age amplifies susceptibility under cumulative fatigue, not from the activity itself

**Rule:** Place speed work (intervals, fast reps) in base/pre-build phase. In build phase, use T-work and MP instead.

### Recovery Run
- **Duration:** 20-40 min
- **Pace:** Very easy, can include walk breaks
- **Purpose:** Blood flow, active recovery
- **When:** Day after hard workout or race

## Part 4: Age Philosophy

> **Age is a variable, not a limiter.**

### What The Research Says
- VO2max declines ~10% per decade after 30
- Recovery takes longer with age
- Injury risk increases

### What The Data Shows
- PRs at 57 are possible with proper training
- Age-based expectations are often wrong for individuals
- What matters: cumulative fatigue, recovery patterns, injury history — not birth year

### The StrideIQ Approach

| Factor | For Younger Athletes | For Older Athletes |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Recovery days | Can often do back-to-back hard | May need more recovery between quality |
| Speed work timing | Can handle intervals in build | Speed work in BASE only; T-work in build |
| Weekly structure | Can combine intensity + hard long | Pick one: weekly intensity OR hard long |
| Strides/Hills | Good | ESSENTIAL — safer than intervals, maintains speed |

**The Rule:**  
Age informs initial assumptions IF no other data exists. Once individual data exists, it overrides age-based predictions.

## Part 5: The Cumulative Fatigue Principle

### The Insight
Race day, you run 26.2 miles on TIRED legs. Training 20 miles on fresh legs doesn't simulate that. But training 16 miles after days of consistent running DOES.

### Application
- The fatigue accumulated before the long run IS the training
- Strategic weekly volume matters more than single long runs
- The long run is the capstone, not the foundation

### The Balance
- Cumulative fatigue builds fitness
- But TOO MUCH cumulative fatigue causes injury
- Cut-back weeks prevent overreach
- Monitor for signs: declining efficiency, persistent soreness, mood changes

## Part 6: The 80/20 Distribution

### The Principle
80% of training at low intensity, 20% at moderate-to-high intensity.

### The Why
- Elite athletes across ALL endurance sports converge on this independently
- This isn't coincidence — it's the body's optimal adaptation pattern
- 80% low intensity allows recovery between quality sessions
- The 20% high intensity is more effective BECAUSE of the 80% easy

### The Definition
- Low intensity = below ventilatory threshold (can talk comfortably)
- Moderate = threshold zone (short phrases)
- High = above threshold (can't talk)

### The Trap
Recreational runners tend toward 50/50 (too much moderate intensity). This creates chronic fatigue without the recovery to adapt.

## Part 7: Individualization Framework

### Speed vs Endurance Responders

Some runners respond better to speed work, others to volume. This isn't a fixed identity — it applies to periodization:

- **In Base Phase:** Respond to speed work (strides, hills, short intervals)
- **In Build Phase:** Respond to endurance + threshold + MP work

**Application:**  
Match the stimulus to the phase, not to a fixed "type."

### Volume-Appropriate Prescription

| Weekly Volume | Appropriate Workouts |
|---------------|---------------------|
| <30 mpw | Easy running, strides, occasional T-work (short) |
| 30-50 mpw | T-work, MP in long runs, medium-long runs |
| 50-70 mpw | Full T-blocks, MP long runs, medium-long runs |
| 70+ mpw | All of the above, plus intervals in base phase |

**Rule:** A 25 mpw runner should NOT do a 20-mile long run. A 40 mpw runner should NOT do 40-min continuous tempo.

## Part 8: Injury Prevention

### The Pattern
Injuries occur when intensity is introduced under high cumulative fatigue.

### Prevention Rules
1. **New stress on fresh legs** — introduce new workout types early in training cycles
2. **Speed work in base phase** — legs are fresh, cumulative fatigue is low
3. **No intervals mid-build** — cumulative fatigue is too high
4. **Cut-back weeks are mandatory** — every 3rd or 4th week
5. **Never increase volume AND intensity simultaneously**

### The Hierarchy
When something has to give:
1. Protect consistency (keep running)
2. Protect volume (don't add intensity at cost of miles)
3. Protect easy days (don't make them moderate)
