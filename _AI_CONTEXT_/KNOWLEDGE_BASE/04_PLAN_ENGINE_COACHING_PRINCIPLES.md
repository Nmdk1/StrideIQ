## 1. Long Run Caps — Distance Ceilings by Event

### The Rule

| Event | Peak LR Cap | Exception |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Ultra (50K+) | No distance cap — volume-capacity driven | Volume floor gate applies |
| Marathon | 22 miles | Sub-elite (sub-2:20 MP equivalent): ONE instance at 24mi per build, rest capped at 22 |
| Half marathon | 18 miles | None |
| 10K | 18 miles | None |
| 5K | 18 miles | None |

Long runs must NOT flatline at their cap. The staircase should visit peak distance, cut back, potentially revisit once, then taper. Parking at 18 every week for a 10K plan is repetitive stress, not progressive overload.

### The Why

Three principles converge to create these caps:

**Biomechanical cost rises exponentially with duration.** Davis separates training load into physiological, biomechanical, and psychological. The aerobic return from a long run plateaus around 2.5-3 hours for most runners, but connective tissue damage, impact accumulation, and glycogen depletion cost keep climbing. Past 22 miles for a marathon runner, you're buying diminishing physiological return at exponentially increasing injury risk.

**The Pfitzinger evidence.** His plan tiers show the ceilings clearly: 20-21 miles for ≤55mpw athletes, 22-23 for 70-85mpw, 23+ only for 85+ mpw elite. Even elite athletes hit 23+ only once or twice in a cycle. 22 is the practical ceiling for all but the highest-volume runners.

**For non-marathon distances, the long run's role changes.** A 10K runner doesn't need marathon-level glycogen depletion — their race is 35-50 minutes. The long run serves aerobic support: capillarization, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation enzymes. 18 miles gives 2+ hours of sustained aerobic stimulus. Going past 18 creates recovery debt that steals from threshold and interval sessions — the sessions that actually determine shorter-distance performance.

### When the Cap Engages

The cap is applied AFTER volume-capacity computation. If an athlete's volume supports a 25-mile long run (e.g., 75mpw experienced runner) but they're training for a marathon, cap at 22. Their volume capacity doesn't change what the event demands; the excess capacity means their 22-miler is well within their absorption range — which is ideal.

## 2. Marathon-Pace Long Runs — Substantial, Progressive

### The Rule

Marathon plans must include MP long runs that progress from introductory (6-8 miles at MP) to race-validating (14-16 miles at MP) across the build. 6 miles at MP is an introduction — not training. It's "touching it so you know what it feels like."

### The Progression

| Phase | MP Segment | Total Run | Purpose |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| General (late) | 6-8 mi at MP | 14-16 mi total | Introduction — calibration, neuromuscular patterning |
| Supportive | 8-10 mi at MP | 16-18 mi total | Development — extending time at race effort |
| Specific (early) | 10-12 mi at MP | 18-20 mi total | Validation — proving the pace is sustainable |
| Specific (late) | 14-16 mi at MP | 18-22 mi total | Race rehearsal — confidence and final fuel practice |

Structure: easy warmup (3-4mi) → MP segment → easy cooldown (2-3mi). The MP segment is continuous — not intervals, not alternating. Sustained race-pace effort.

### The Why

**Marathon pace is the most important race-specific stimulus.** Pfitzinger ranks glycogen storage/fat utilization and running economy as #2 and #3 marathon success factors. Extended MP work trains both simultaneously — it teaches the body to burn fuel at race pace and grooves the neuromuscular pattern.

**The volume of MP work must be proportional to the race demand.** You're asking your body to hold MP for 26.2 miles. If the longest you've ever sustained it is 6 miles, you haven't prepared for the physiological or psychological challenge. Pfitzinger's plans progress from 8mi at MP in Block 1 to 14mi at MP in Block 3.

**Canova's extension of intensity.** Start at race-specific speed, gradually extend duration. The speed is constant. The adaptation comes from making the body sustain it longer.

**Green's evidence.** 8-12 miles at or around marathon pace embedded in 14-18 mile long runs for Seidel. High volume of race-specific work at controlled intensity.

### Event Gating

MP long runs are ONLY for marathon plans. Half marathon and shorter distances get their race-specific work through mid-week quality sessions (threshold for HM, VO2max for 10K/5K), not through the long run.

## 3. Medium-Long Runs — The Aerobic Volume Multiplier

### The Rule

Mid-week aerobic runs of 10-14 miles (55-70% of long run distance), placed mid-week, at easy effort. Hard cap: 15 miles. Not for everyone — gated by weekly volume and experience.

### The Why

**The gap between easy runs and long runs.** If easy runs are 5-7 miles and the long run is 16-20, there's a massive aerobic development opportunity being missed mid-week. The medium-long fills that gap with sustained aerobic stimulus without the full recovery cost of a second long run.

**Dose-dependent aerobic adaptation.** Mitochondrial biogenesis, capillary density, and fat oxidation enzyme activity scale with time at aerobic effort. Two sustained aerobic sessions per week (long run + medium-long) produce substantially more adaptation than one long run plus several 40-minute easy runs.

**Pfitzinger made this a named workout category.** 70-75% of long run distance, same intensity, mid-week. His 85+ plans prescribe 14-16 mile medium-longs.

**The 15-mile hard cap exists for a reason.** Past 15, the medium-long becomes a second long run, which doubles recovery debt and undermines quality session absorption. The medium-long should enhance the week, not dominate it.

### Gating Rules

| Weekly Volume | Medium-Long Status | Size |
|---------------|-------------------|------|
| < 40 mpw | No medium-long | N/A |
| 40-55 mpw | Optional, 10-11 mi | 55-60% of LR |
| 55-70 mpw | Standard, 11-13 mi | 60-65% of LR |
| 70+ mpw | Full, 13-15 mi | 65-70% of LR |

Beginners: no medium-long regardless of volume. The aerobic base isn't deep enough to absorb it without compromising recovery.

### Weekly Structure Implication

Medium-long + threshold + intervals + long run in the same week is four substantive sessions. That's not for everyone. But for athletes who can absorb it, it's the aerobic volume multiplier that separates competitive from recreational training.

### Placement

Mid-week (typically Wednesday or Thursday), at least 2 days after the primary quality session and 2-3 days before the long run. The medium-long is easy effort — it should not compete with quality sessions for recovery resources.

## 4. The MP Litmus Test — Pace Validation

### The Rule

If an athlete can't sustain their goal MP for 14-16 miles by the end of their plan, the pace is wrong. The system should flag this, not override the athlete's choice.

### The Why

**Durability is the key.** Davis and Roche define physiological resilience as the ability to resist degradation of start-line physiology during prolonged exercise. Cardiac drift, glycogen depletion, neuromuscular fatigue — if these overwhelm the athlete at mile 12 of MP training work, they will overwhelm them at mile 20 of the race. Race-day adrenaline doesn't bridge a 10-mile fitness gap.

**Progressive validation.** Each MP milestone is a checkpoint:
- 6-8mi at MP: introduction — did they find the pace?
- 10-12mi at MP: development — is cardiac drift controlled?
- 14-16mi at MP: validation — this IS their marathon pace

If the athlete nails 12 at MP with controlled cardiac drift, the pace is confirmed. If they struggle at 10, the system raises the flag early enough to adjust.

### System Behavior

The system INFORMS, the athlete DECIDES (Principle 4). The flag is informational:
> "Your longest sustained MP effort was 10 miles with 6.8% cardiac drift. Your training suggests your current sustainable MP may correspond to approximately X:XX. Consider whether your goal pace needs adjustment."

This connects to the Pre-Race Fingerprint concept: a quantified readiness signal, not a prescription.

## 5. Threshold Format Progression — Not Just Intervals

### The Rule

Threshold work progresses from cruise intervals in the general phase to continuous tempo in the specific phase. The format IS the progression.

### The Progression

| Phase | Format | Structure | Purpose |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------|
| General (early) | Cruise intervals | 6×5min w/ 2min jog | Introduce threshold stimulus with recovery breaks |
| General (late) | Longer intervals | 4×8min w/ 2min jog | Extend time at threshold |
| Supportive | Extended intervals | 3×10min w/ 3min → 2×15min w/ 3min | Bridge toward continuous |
| Specific | Continuous tempo | 25-40min unbroken | Race-relevant sustained sub-SSmax effort |

### The Why

**SSmax is the boundary.** Davis defines Steady-State Maximum as the boundary between sustainable and unsustainable intensities. Threshold work should be sub-SSmax — controlled. The continuous tempo trains the body and mind to sustain sub-SSmax effort without breaks, which is exactly what racing demands.

**The Norwegian model validates this.** 2×25-30min at 2.3-3.0 mmol/L lactate — essentially continuous threshold, producing massive aerobic adaptation with minimal muscle damage. Roche adapts this as one threshold morning plus one structured long run.

**Extension of intensity applied to threshold.** Same speed, longer duration, fewer breaks, across the build. The athlete earns the continuous tempo by proving they can handle the intervals first. This is Canova/Davis applied to threshold.

## 6. Governing Integration Principles

These rules override any individual workout decision:

1. **N=1 always.** Volume caps, MP progression, medium-long sizing — all derived from the athlete's data, not population tables. The tables above are starting points; the athlete's history adjusts them.

2. **Never three quality days.** Two quality mid-week sessions + a quality long run = three quality days. When the long run is a workout (MP long, fatigue resistance, etc.), cap mid-week quality at 1. Structure A/B alternation.

3. **Recovery resources are finite.** Adding a medium-long means something else gets easier. The weekly total must stay within the 80/20 distribution. Quality volume limits: VO2max ≤ 8% weekly mileage or 10K total; threshold ≤ 10-15% weekly mileage.

4. **The staircase has rhythm.** Build, cutback, build, peak, cutback, taper. Peak distances are visited, not maintained. Every 3-4 weeks, the body gets a genuine cutback (60-65% of cycle peak) to absorb and adapt.

5. **Phase purpose is non-negotiable.** General = full spectrum aerobic base. Supportive = bridge toward race-specific. Specific = race-pace dominated. Don't put continuous 40min tempo in the general phase. Don't put VO2max pyramids in the specific phase of a marathon build.
