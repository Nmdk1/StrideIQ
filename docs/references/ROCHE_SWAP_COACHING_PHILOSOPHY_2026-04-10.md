## Background

David Roche: Two-time national champion (10K), 2014 USATF Trail Runner
of the Year, 2024 Leadville 100 course record. Former 200-210 lb
football/baseball player who became an elite distance runner. Co-runs
SWAP (Some Work, All Play) with Megan Roche (MD, PhD). Coaches elite
and amateur athletes through Patreon-based training plans.

## Core Philosophy

### The N=1 Problem

Roche articulates the fundamental epistemological challenge of coaching
better than anyone in the field:

> "The hardest part of running training theory is that every athlete is
> their own N=1 study... the REAL PATTERNS LOOK THE SAME AS THE
> SPURIOUS CORRELATIONS AT FIRST."

He gives a personal example: he stopped doing doubles after his son
was born and his ultra performances took off. Then he reintroduced
doubles and demolished his Leadville time. Which is the signal?

> "We will never know for sure, which is why a holistic view is so
> important."

**Implication for StrideIQ:** This is the honest framing the correlation
engine and Operating Manual should adopt. When presenting findings, the
system should communicate uncertainty: "This pattern appears in your
data, but it could be coincidental. Here's what we know and what we
don't." StrideIQ's advantage over a single coach is that it can
aggregate across athletes — turning N=1 noise into N=many signal —
but it should never overclaim on individual correlations.

### Joy-First, Not Suffering-First

The SWAP name — "Some Work, All Play" — is not branding. It's
philosophy. Running is optional. Doubles are optional. If you're
dreading it, skip it. If it affects sleep, family, or work, bag it.

> "Doubles, like run training in the first place, is all about
> exploring the limits of your potential by doing something that
> seems moderately unreasonable."

**Implication for coach model voice:** The coach should never sound
punitive or disappointed when an athlete skips a workout. "You skipped
Thursday's threshold — good call if you weren't feeling it. Here's
how we adjust" is the right tone. Not "You missed an important session."

### Pattern Recognition Is Coaching

> "Coaching is a process of pattern-recognition. You'll see so many
> superfluous patterns, but the trickiest part is that the real
> patterns look the same as the spurious correlations at first."

This is what StrideIQ's correlation engine IS — pattern recognition
across training data. Roche does this manually with experience across
hundreds of athletes. StrideIQ does it computationally. Same process,
different scale.

## The Theory of Easy Doubles

### The Evidence

- 2019 JSCR study: Easy run volume had the highest correlation with
  performance (r=0.72) across 85 elite athletes' first 7 years
- 2020 EJSS study: Confirmed same finding
- Ingebrigtsen brothers: 150-160 km/week across 13-14 sessions
- Canova athletes: Block training days with two hard workouts plus
  easy doubles
- Molly Seidel: 6 doubles per week before Olympic medal
- 2025 Strava marathon study: Volume of easy training predicted
  performance; higher intensity proportions had INVERSE relationship

### Why Doubles, Not Longer Singles

The key insight: **in running, WE are the machine, and we lose
efficiency across longer runs.** A recent running economy study showed
big reductions beginning as early as 1 hour in many athletes and
2 hours in pros.

In cycling, the bike is the machine — it doesn't lose drivetrain
efficiency. That's why cyclists do big singles and runners split
volume.

> "Doubles help solve the math equation."

Accumulate aerobic volume while managing biomechanical load. The total
volume matters for adaptation; the distribution across sessions manages
injury risk and efficiency degradation.

### Beyond Volume: The Hormonal Stimulus

Doubles may provide benefits beyond simple volume addition:
- Hormonal stimulus that enhances adaptation
- Increased adaptation markers and protein expression
- Improved glycogen replenishment
- Possible epigenetic signaling
- 2012 mouse study: 3×10min runs = same adaptations as 30min
  continuous, with possibly larger increases in TSP-1 expression

### Trail vs Road: Why Doubles Are Less Common in Ultras

Roche offers two theories:

1. **Trail running relies on musculoskeletal resilience to variable
   loading patterns.** The track is an aerobic contest; trails add
   musculoskeletal damage from variable terrain. Longer singles create
   "resilient monsters" (if they don't break). This connects directly
   to Andy Jones's resilience/durability research.

2. **Margins in trail running are wider.** A 0.1% aerobic improvement
   may be swallowed by a 10% improvement from not falling on a descent.
   The optimization pressure that drives track athletes to find every
   advantage is less intense on trails.

### SWAP Guidelines for Doubles

1. **Keep it very easy** — up to 2× your 5K pace. No pace is too slow.
   Roche does his at 8-10 min/mile without GPS. "I have seen some pro
   runners in Boulder doing them at what looks like 10 minute pace."

2. **Keep it short** — 20-30 minutes. Evidence suggests the productive
   hormonal stimulus rises most rapidly in the first 30 minutes before
   leveling off. Up to 45 min for high-volume athletes.

3. **At least a few hours after first activity** — glycogen
   replenishment needs time. 3+ hours is a good rule.

4. **Add on workout days first, aerobic days next** — easy doubles on
   workout days may maximize adaptation. Don't double on long run days
   (glycogen overwhelm).

5. **All doubles are optional** — "There is no such thing as a
   mandatory double for our athletes."

   > "Are you dreading it? Skip. Do you have any niggles, even the
   > smallest whisper from a gnat's ass? Chill. Affect sleep, family,
   > work? Bag it."

## Implications for StrideIQ

### Plan Generator

- **Doubles as optional add-ons:** The generator should offer optional
  easy doubles on workout days for athletes above a volume threshold
  (e.g., >60 km/wk). These appear on the calendar as "Optional: Easy
  Double — 20-30min very easy" and can be toggled on/off.

- **Volume accumulation without biomechanical risk:** For athletes
  wanting to increase volume, doubles are safer than longer singles.
  The generator should prefer prescribing a double over extending a
  single run past the point of efficiency degradation (~60-90 min
  for most athletes, ~120 min for advanced).

- **No doubles on long run days.** Hard rule.

### Coach Model

- **Voice and personality:** Roche's writing voice — funny, self-
  deprecating, honest about uncertainty, scientifically grounded but
  never pompous — is the voice the coach model should emulate. Not
  a textbook. Not a drill sergeant. A smart friend who runs.

- **Uncertainty communication:** When the coach model references
  correlations, it should acknowledge the N=1 limitation: "Your data
  suggests X, but we're working with limited signal here. Let's see
  if it holds over the next few weeks."

- **Permission to skip:** The coach model should actively give
  permission to skip workouts. "Rest when you need it" is coaching,
  not weakness.

### Trail/Ultra Mode (Future)

- **Resilience as the primary training target:** For trail/ultra plans,
  long singles become MORE important (not less) because musculoskeletal
  resilience to variable terrain is the key limiter — not aerobic
  capacity.

- **Effort-based, not pace-based:** Trail workouts should prescribe
  effort and duration, not pace. Pace is meaningless on variable
  terrain.

- **Doubles serve a different purpose:** In trail/ultra, doubles are
  more about cross-training (treadmill hill, bike) than about
  running volume accumulation.

### Correlation Engine

- **The "spurious vs real pattern" problem:** Roche names the exact
  problem the correlation engine faces. StrideIQ's advantage is scale
  — aggregate across athletes to separate signal from noise. But for
  any INDIVIDUAL athlete's Operating Manual, findings should carry
  confidence indicators, not false certainty.

## The Beginner Ultra Plan: A Masterclass in Accessible Coaching

The SWAP Beginner 50K-50 Mile plan is the clearest expression of
Roche's philosophy applied to the least experienced athletes. It
reveals principles the advanced marathon plan takes for granted.

### Three Ultra Principles for Beginners

1. **Hike fast on uphills** — walking is not resting, it's a different
   movement pattern. Arms pump, pace is purposeful.
2. **Run the downhills** — "downhills are free speed." Steeper and
   faster than race day in training to prevent rhabdomyolysis.
3. **Move with purpose on flats** — no lingering, no shuffling.

### Structural Innovations

**Run/hike combinations as the base unit:** Every run is prescribed as
"20-40 min easy run followed by fast hiking to reach 1 hour." The run
builds running fitness; the fast hike adds time-on-feet without
biomechanical load. The total activity time is the target, not the
running distance.

**Time-based, not distance-based (mostly):** Weekday sessions are in
minutes. Only the long runs use miles. This removes pace anxiety
entirely — a beginner can't fail a time-based session.

**Uphill treadmill as a key tool:** Introduced in week 6, the
"star of the show." Set to a grade where you can hike faster than
race cutoff pace (≥3 mph). This builds specific power for race-day
climbs in a controlled, low-impact environment.

**Speed Legs strength routine, periodized:** Appears 1-2x/week
through week 8, then drops for race-specific adaptation. Starts
with no added weight. Deliberately causes soreness early ("you'll
probably still be sore from Speed Legs, and that's ok") so the
athlete learns to train through mild discomfort.

**Descending taper:** Weeks 11-12 cut volume dramatically. Week 12
race week: 15min easy + 4×30s hills + 10min moderate + 5min easy
on Wednesday. That's the final stimulus — tiny, sharp, confidence-
building.

### Volume Progression

| Week | Long run | Total character |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | 6 mi run/hike | Introduction |
| 2 | 8 mi run/hike | Building |
| 3 | 10 mi run/hike | Building |
| 4 | 8 mi (down) | Recovery |
| 5 | 10 mi | Rebuild |
| 6 | 14 mi | Step up |
| 7 | 12 mi (+ 8mi Sun) | Volume weekend |
| 8 | 16 mi (+ 10mi Sun) | Big weekend |
| 9 | 20 mi | Peak long run |
| 10 | 14 mi (+ 10mi) | Step back |
| 11 | 8 mi (+ 6mi) | Taper |
| 12 | RACE | Race |

### Speed Work Progression (Even for Beginners)

Roche includes speed work from week 2 — even for beginners:
- W2: 6×30s hills with walk-down
- W3: 3×90s + 3×45s hills
- W4: 10×30s flat fast/90s easy
- W6: 10×1min fast/2min easy
- W7: 4×2min + 4×1min hills
- W8: 12×1min fast/90s easy
- W9: 5×3min hills mod/hard
- W10: 5K time trial
- W11: 1hr moderate continuous

This IS extension-based progression: 30s → 90s → 1min → 2min → 3min.
Same effort (5K-10K), longer holds. Even at the beginner level.

### The Voice

- "No single day matters that much in the big scheme of growth"
- "Think 'kid at recess,' running with joy"
- "Just Say No To Rhabdo!"
- "Now we're getting SPICY!!! Embrace that space!"
- "I bet this one feels surprisingly good!"
- "CELEBRATION AND PIZZA. Pizza is not a requirement, but it's strongly encouraged."
- "WE ARE SO PROUD OF YOU!!!!"

This is the voice the coach model should learn from. Not every
interaction needs to be analytical. Sometimes the right coaching
response is "WE GOT THIS."

### Implications for StrideIQ

**1. Run/hike as a workout structure:** For beginner and ultra plans,
the generator should support run/hike combinations: "30min easy run +
30min fast hiking." The `segments` schema needs a `"type": "hike"`
segment with effort-based prescription.

**2. Time-based prescription for beginners:** Athletes below a
threshold (e.g., <30 km/week or <6 months of running) should get
time-based workouts, not distance-based. "Run 30 minutes easy" is
less intimidating than "Run 5K."

**3. The uphill treadmill option:** For athletes without trails or
hills, the generator should suggest treadmill hiking at specific
grades as an alternative to outdoor hill work.

**4. The pizza principle:** The post-race celebration is part of the
plan. The generator could include a race-day workout with a
description that ends with encouragement and celebration, not
just splits.

## Long-Term Base Building Plan: The Blueprint for Build Mode

The SWAP Long-Term Base Building plan solves the problem most training
platforms ignore: **what do you do when there's no race on the
calendar?** This plan is designed to be run indefinitely, producing
continuous aerobic improvement month after month. It is the single
most important reference for StrideIQ's Build mode generator.

### Architecture

**10-week initial plan + repeatable 6-week cycle + periodic bonus week.**

- Weeks 1-4: Introduction with threshold work, hill strides, and
  progressive volume. Establishes the rhythm.
- Weeks 5-10: The repeatable core. Designed to be run "as many times
  as wanted, ideally increasing volume and improving paces slightly
  on workouts and easy running."
- Week 11 (BONUS WEEK): A vVO2 session (12-20 × 1min fast/1min easy)
  and a hard tempo long run. Inserted every 4-6 weeks to prevent
  aerobic ceiling and stimulate top-end adaptation.

**When an athlete repeats:** They restart at week 5, not week 1.
Weeks 1-4 were the onramp; weeks 5-10 are the engine. The athlete
bumps volume slightly (within the prescribed ranges) and paces
slightly each cycle.

### Distance Ranges: Multiple Plans in One

The signature innovation. Every workout uses ranges:

| Day | Range | Beginner | Advanced |
|-----|-------|----------|----------|
| Tuesday | 3-12 mi easy | 3 mi | 12 mi |
| Saturday | 8-20 mi easy | 8 mi | 20 mi |
| Sunday | 4-14 mi easy | 4 mi | 14 mi |
| **Weekly total** | **~16 mi** | **~100 mi** |

The same plan structure serves a 20 mpw beginner and a 100 mpw
advanced athlete. The athlete self-selects within the range based on
"low end: beginner or intermediate; high end: advanced athletes with
big base."

**Implication for generator:** The generator should offer distance
ranges for Build mode, not fixed distances. For a given athlete's
current weekly volume, the system places them at the appropriate
point in each range and nudges slightly upward each cycle. The
athlete sees "8-12 miles easy" and can self-select based on how
they feel. This is the "plans written in pencil" principle in
action — the structure is fixed, the volume is athlete-determined.

### Weekly Structure (The Seven-Day Pattern)

| Day | Role | Character |
|-----|------|-----------|
| Monday | Rest + heat | Recovery. Sauna/hot tub for blood volume. Advanced: optional Z1 x-train. |
| Tuesday | Easy run + hills | Volume accumulation. Hill strides (5×20-30s) for economy/power. Optional x-train double. |
| Wednesday | KEY SESSION | Alternates: Threshold (uphill TM preferred) or steady/moderate run. The only structured intensity. |
| Thursday | Cross-training | 60-120 min x-train OR easy running. "Accumulate Z2 time." High carb fueling for 90+ min. |
| Friday | Easy run | Shortest run of the week. "Keep this extra easy going into the weekend!" |
| Saturday | Long run | Easy or easy/mod over hills. Fueling emphasis. The bread and butter. |
| Sunday | Aerobic flex | Easy run + hills, or long x-train/hike. "Accumulate some volume without too much stress." |

**Key structural observations:**

1. **Only ONE quality session per week (Wednesday).** Everything else
   is easy or easy/mod. This is 85/15 polarization at its purest for
   base building. The remaining 6 days are aerobic accumulation.

2. **Cross-training is a first-class day, not filler.** Thursday is
   always x-train (bike, elliptical, swim). Not "rest or cross-train"
   — it's prescribed as a training day with Z2 targets. This reduces
   running impact while maintaining aerobic load.

3. **Hill strides everywhere.** 4-5 × 20-30 second hills appear on
   Tuesday, Saturday, or Sunday in most weeks. Running economy
   maintenance without structured speed work.

4. **Heat training on rest days.** Every Monday mentions sauna or hot
   tub. Blood volume adaptation on days when the body is recovering —
   free physiological gains.

5. **Sunday as a flex day.** Can be running, x-train, hiking, or a
   long adventure. "You can do long x-training or hiking days here."
   This prevents burnout by letting the athlete do what sounds fun.

### Wednesday Threshold Progression (Extension-Based)

The Wednesday session alternates between threshold and steady/moderate
across the 10 weeks:

| Week | Type | Structure |
|------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Hills | 5-10 × 30s slight hills fast |
| 2 | **Threshold** | 3-6 × 5min @ 1-hour effort, 2min recovery |
| 3 | Steady | 6-12 mi easy/moderate to moderate |
| 4 | **Threshold** | 4-8 × 5min @ 1-hour effort, 2min recovery |
| 5 | Easy | 6-14 mi easy (aerobic consolidation) |
| 6 | **Threshold** | 4-10 × 5min @ 1-hour effort, 2min recovery |
| 7 | Steady | 6-14 mi easy/moderate to moderate |
| 8 | **Threshold** | 6-12 × 5min @ 1-hour effort, 2min recovery |
| 9 | Easy | 6-14 mi easy (aerobic consolidation) |
| 10 | **Threshold** | 3-6 × 10min @ 1-hour effort, 3min recovery |
| 11 | **BONUS: vVO2** | 12-20 × 1min fast/1min easy + hard tempo |

**The extension is unmistakable:**
- Weeks 2→4→6→8: 3-6 reps → 4-8 → 4-10 → 6-12 (same 5min intervals,
  growing rep count)
- Week 10: The jump — 5min intervals become **10min intervals**
  (3-6 × 10min). Same effort, double the rep length.
- BONUS WEEK: Completely different stimulus (1min vVO2) to touch
  the top of the aerobic system.

This is EXACTLY the pattern the generator should implement for Build
mode threshold progression. The effort stays constant ("1-hour
effort" = threshold). The volume grows by increasing reps. Then the
rep duration doubles while the rep count resets. Then a periodic
bonus week hits the top end.

### Uphill Treadmill as Preferred Threshold Tool

Roche prescribes threshold on the uphill treadmill over flat running:

> "On the uphill treadmill, aim to be 4+ mph using running form, at
> whatever grade leads to Z2 running"

For threshold: "60-105 min with 3-6 × 5 min around 1-hour effort."

**Why the treadmill?**
- Controlled grade reduces impact vs. flat running at threshold pace
- Forces running form at lower speeds (less biomechanical load)
- Builds specific climbing power (transferable to any terrain)
- The "if no uphill treadmill" alternative is always provided

**Implication for generator:** For Build mode, the generator should
offer indoor/outdoor alternatives for threshold sessions. Athletes
with treadmill access get the uphill version; others get the flat
running version with slightly different rep structures.

### Long Run Philosophy

Saturday long runs use "easy/mod over hills" — not a specific pace.

> "Easy/mod isn't a specific effort, but a general cue to do
> intentional running. Any long run can involve unstructured efforts
> or even segment chases when it seems fun."

This is the opposite of rigid pace prescription. The athlete is told
to run intentionally, and to push when it feels right. "Segment
chases when it seems fun" — Strava segments as organic fartlek.

The progression: 6→8→8→8→8→8→10→10→8→10 miles at the low end.
At the high end: 14→16→16→16→20→16→20→18→16→20.

**Recovery patterning in the long run:** Weeks 4, 9 are down weeks
(8 mi at low end, 16 mi at high end). Not every week builds. The
plan breathes.

### The BONUS WEEK: Preventing the Aerobic Ceiling

> "If you repeat the plan, insert the BONUS WEEK every 4-6 weeks,
> which will ensure that the plan leads to long-term, month-after-
> month improvement."

The BONUS WEEK has two unique sessions:

1. **Wednesday vVO2:** 12-20 × 1min fast (5K effort) / 1min easy.
   This is the only session in the entire plan that goes above
   threshold. It exists to stimulate VO2max and prevent the aerobic
   system from plateauing at the threshold ceiling.

2. **Saturday hard tempo:** 10-16 mi easy/mod with 30-60 minutes
   mod/hard to hard. This is the "put the aerobic and muscular
   adaptations to work" session — a race-simulation effort within
   a long run.

**Implication for generator:** Build mode should auto-insert a
"stimulus week" every 4-6 weeks of repeating blocks. This week
contains one vVO2 session and one tempo long run. Without this,
the athlete will plateau. This is the equivalent of the founder
never needing to manually schedule a deload or stimulus — the
system handles the cadence.

### Cross-Training Prescription Depth

Thursday is always x-train with specific intensity guidance:

> "Aim to accumulate a lot of Z2 time on cross training, which may
> mean pushing a bit more than purely easy for advanced athletes."

> "Fuel cross training and any 90+ minute activity with high carb
> intake to improve aerobic adaptation"

This isn't "take a day off and maybe ride." It's a prescribed Z2
aerobic session on a non-impact modality with fueling requirements.

**Implication for generator:** Build mode should include x-train
days as prescribed sessions, not rest days. The workout description
should specify: "60-90 min bike/elliptical at Z2 heart rate. Fuel
with carbs if >60 min." The coach model should track x-train
compliance the same as run compliance.

### Optional Doubles (Tier System)

The plan introduces doubles gradually:

- Weeks 1-2: "Optional doubles just involve Z1/Z2 bike spinning,
  elliptical, swim, uphill hike"
- Week 3+: "Starting on this Wednesday, you could add a cross
  training double"
- Weeks 5+: "Optional 30-45 min easy x-train or light jog for
  advanced athletes" — the JOG option appears only after 4 weeks
  of x-train doubles

**Implication for generator:** Doubles should unlock progressively.
First x-train doubles, then easy jog doubles, only after the athlete
has adapted to the base volume pattern. The generator should never
prescribe run doubles in week 1 of a Build plan.

### Implications for StrideIQ Build Mode (Summary)

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Distance ranges | Prescribe ranges, not fixed distances. System places athlete in range based on weekly volume. |
| Repeatable blocks | Weeks 5-10 as a 6-week cycle. Auto-renew with slight volume/pace bump. |
| BONUS WEEK cadence | Auto-insert vVO2 + tempo long run every 4-6 weeks. |
| Single quality day | Wednesday only. Everything else is aerobic accumulation. |
| Cross-training as training | Thursday x-train is a prescribed session, not filler. |
| Uphill TM threshold | Offer treadmill threshold as default; flat running as fallback. |
| Hill strides throughout | 4-5 × 20-30s hills on 2-3 days/week. Economy maintenance. |
| Heat training | Monday rest + heat prescription. |
| Progressive doubles | X-train doubles first → jog doubles for advanced only. |
| Self-selection within structure | "Easy/mod" and ranges give athlete agency within the plan's framework. |

## The 5K/10K Speed Plan: Economy-Focused Race Preparation

The SWAP 5K/10K Speed Training Plan is the short-race counterpart to
everything else in the KB. 8 weeks, focused on running economy and
VO2max, with the same distance-range architecture. This plan reveals
how SWAP applies the same structural principles across the full
distance spectrum — from ultra to 5K — while shifting the
physiological emphasis.

### Architecture

**8 weeks. ONE primary quality session per week (Wednesday). Race
in week 8.**

Shorter than the marathon (12 weeks) and ultra (12 weeks) plans
because 5K/10K fitness has a shorter development window. The
aerobic base is assumed or built via the base building plan; this
plan converts it to speed.

### Distance Ranges (Again)

Same "three plans in one" concept:

| Band | Weekly range | Peak |
|------|-------------|------|
| Low (intermediate) | ~18-30 mi | ~40 mi |
| Mid (advanced) | ~40-60 mi | ~70 mi |
| High (super advanced) | ~60-90 mi | ~101 mi |

> "Think of it as 3 different plans in one! Low end of range =
> intermediate. Middle of range = advanced. Top of range = super
> duper advanced athletes who prefer higher volume."

### Weekly Structure

| Day | Role |
|-----|------|
| Monday | Rest |
| Tuesday | Easy run + strides (flat) or hills |
| **Wednesday** | **KEY SESSION (speed or threshold)** |
| Thursday | Easy run or x-train (recovery from Wednesday) |
| Friday | Easy run or rest (pre-long-run ease) |
| **Saturday** | **Long run with structured element** |
| Sunday | Easy + hills and/or x-train flex |

**Critical difference from base building:** Saturday long runs are
NOT purely easy. Every Saturday includes a structured element:
fartlek, threshold tempo, or fatigue resistance effort. This makes
Saturday a second quality stimulus — appropriate for race prep
where the athlete needs to convert aerobic fitness to race fitness.

### Wednesday Speed Progression

| Week | Structure | Effort | Physiological target |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | 4×(4×1min fast/1min easy), 2min between sets | 5K | VO2max introduction |
| 2 | 4×(3/2/1min fast), 1min/3min rest | 10K | Aerobic speed |
| 3 | 3×(30/60/90/60/30s fast), 1min/3min rest | 5K | VO2max pyramid |
| 4 | 6×2min hills mod/hard + 5×45s hills fast | 5K/3K | Neuromuscular power |
| 5 | 3×(3×90s fast/90s easy), 3min between sets | 5K (push hard) | VO2max accumulation |
| 6 | 6×5min @ 1hr effort + 4×30s fast | Threshold + speed | Big aerobic session |
| 7 | 1mi hard, 6×1/1 @ 5K, 1mi @ 1hr effort | Race simulation | Full-spectrum rehearsal |
| 8 | 4×2min @ 10K + 4×1min @ 3K | 10K/3K | Nervous system prime |

**Progression pattern:** This is NOT pure extension like the marathon
or base plan. For 5K/10K, the progression is **variety-based**: the
interval structure changes every week to hit the aerobic system from
different angles. Extension still exists (1min → 90s → 2min → 5min
durations across the cycle), but the primary progression is
**stimulus novelty** — each week's workout looks different.

This is a key distinction for the generator: marathon plans use
extension (same structure, growing duration). Short-race plans use
**rotation** (different structures weekly, with underlying volume
growth).

### Saturday Long Run Progression (Structured)

| Week | Distance range | Structured element |
|------|---------------|--------------------|
| 1 | 8-16 mi | Fartlek: at 20min and every 5min, 1min @ 1hr effort |
| 2 | 10-18 mi | 4×6min @ 1hr effort, 2min easy |
| 3 | 10-20 mi | Pure easy/mod (aerobic emphasis) |
| 4 | 10-16 mi | 20min moderately hard @ 1hr effort |
| 5 | 10-18 mi | 6min hard near end (fatigue resistance) |
| 6 | 12-20 mi | Pure easy/mod Z2 (aerobic emphasis) |
| 7 | 10-16 mi | 4×5min @ 1hr effort, 90s easy |
| 8 | RACE | 5K/10K race |

**Fatigue resistance (W5):** "Aiming to improve how the body clicks
into carb oxidation after a substantial loading period earlier in
the run." This is a deliberate training stimulus — the easy miles
deplete glycogen stores, then the hard finish forces the body to
access carbohydrate metabolism under fatigue. The generator should
support this workout type: long easy run with a short, hard finish.

### Double Workouts (Advanced Only)

Unlike the base building plan (where doubles are easy), this plan
includes **structured double workouts** on specific weeks:

| Week | Double session |
|------|---------------|
| 1 | Optional: easy run or x-train, 20-35 min |
| 2 | 2-3mi easy, 8-10×1min mod/1min easy (1hr effort), 2-3mi easy |
| 4 | 2-3mi easy, 30/60/90/120/90/60/30s fast (1hr effort + progression), 2-3mi easy |
| 6 | 2-3mi easy, 12-15×1min @ 10K effort/1min easy, 2-3mi easy |

> "These are advanced and risky, but many of you have requested a
> template for how double sessions would work in practice. Only try
> these if you are wanting to test that edge!"

**Implication for generator:** For experienced athletes in Race mode
(5K/10K), the generator can offer optional structured doubles on
Wednesday. These are NOT easy shakeouts — they are threshold or 10K
effort sessions. The generator should mark them with explicit risk
warnings: "Only if 100% healthy and feeling great."

### Effort-Based Pacing (Entire Plan)

No paces are specified ANYWHERE in this plan. Every workout uses
effort language:

- "5K effort" — VO2max work
- "10K effort" — aerobic speed
- "3K effort" — high-end neuromuscular
- "1-hour effort" — threshold
- "800m/mile effort" — stride/economy

> "On flat strides, think smooth and strong, like 800m/mile effort
> rather than a sprint, which should get faster over the course of
> the cycle."

**Implication for generator:** For 5K/10K plans, the generator
should use effort descriptions paired with pace ranges derived from
RPI, not fixed paces. "6×1min fast (5K effort, ~5:30-5:45/mi)" gives
the athlete both the feel target and a pace guideline, without
making pace the primary goal. The athlete learns to run by feel,
which is the skill that matters on race day.

### Terrain Guidance

> "The only rule is to keep strides and the speed workouts on fast
> terrain. Threshold workouts can be on trails though, since the
> emphasis is the aerobic system!"

> "If you're injury prone or over 50, consider doing some of the
> speed workouts on uphills rather than on flats, and you can move
> all strides to be hill strides!"

**Implication for generator:** Workout descriptions should include
terrain guidance. Speed work gets "ideally on flat, fast terrain."
Threshold gets "trails fine." Easy runs get no terrain constraint.
For masters athletes or those with injury history, the generator
should default to hill variants for speed work (less impact, same
neuromuscular stimulus).

### Week 7: The Race Simulation Session

```
1 mile hard → 5min easy → 6×1min fast/1min easy (5K) → 5min easy
→ 1 mile at 1-hour effort → cool down
```

> "With the hard mile, practice all of the pre-race approaches
> you'll use on race day. That effort should make the 1/1s feel
> short (and hard), and the threshold at the end is to prime the
> aerobic system!"

This is a multi-pace session that rehearses the race experience:
the hard mile simulates race start, the 1-minute repeats maintain
VO2 under fatigue, and the threshold finish primes the aerobic
system for race day. The generator should include a "dress rehearsal"
session 10-14 days before any target race.

### Post-Race Continuation

> "CELEBRATION!!! To keep the momentum going, athletes can do a
> long run this day between 10-16 miles! Just nice and easy"

Race day is Sunday. The plan includes an optional long run THE SAME
DAY or the next day. This isn't recovery — it's transition. The
athlete doesn't stop; they flow into whatever comes next.

**Implication for generator:** After a race, the generator should
offer a "transition run" within 24-48 hours. Easy, short, and
explicitly framed as "keeping the momentum going," not training.

### Adaptability to Other Distances

> "This approach would also have athletes ready for a hard mile
> (just make the workout 17 days out 15×1min around mile effort
> with 90s slow recovery) or half marathon (14 days out, do 30min
> moderately hard instead of the listed long run)"

The same 8-week framework can target mile, 5K, 10K, or half
marathon by swapping ONE workout near the end. The structure is
distance-agnostic; only the final specificity changes.

### Implications for StrideIQ Race Mode (5K/10K)

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 8-week duration | 5K/10K plans are shorter than marathon. Generator should auto-select plan length based on race distance. |
| Variety-based progression | Wednesday speed work rotates structures weekly (not pure extension). |
| Structured long runs | Saturday includes fartlek, threshold, or fatigue resistance elements. |
| Effort-based pacing | Efforts ("5K effort") paired with RPI-derived pace ranges, not fixed paces. |
| Terrain guidance | Speed on flat terrain; threshold on any terrain; hills for masters/injury-prone. |
| Structured doubles (advanced) | Optional threshold/10K doubles on Wednesday for experienced athletes. |
| Race simulation | Dress rehearsal session 10-14 days out with multi-pace structure. |
| Fatigue resistance | Long run with hard finish to train carb oxidation under glycogen depletion. |
| Post-race transition | Optional easy long run on race day/next day to maintain momentum. |

## The Champion Ultra Plan: Advanced 50K-50 Mile Race Preparation

The SWAP Champion Plan is the advanced counterpart to the beginner
ultra plan. 12 weeks, designed for intermediate through elite
athletes, with one defining narrative: **"improve power at lactate
threshold, refining that into very fast paces at 90% of threshold
by race day."** This is SSmax development in practice — build the
ceiling, then learn to live closer to it for longer.

### Architecture

**12 weeks. Hybrid progression: speed weeks alternate with threshold
weeks, converging into race-specific simulation.**

Unlike the marathon plan (extension-based) or the 5K plan
(variety-based), this plan uses **alternating emphasis**: speed
one week, threshold the next, with both progressing in parallel.
The physiological logic: ultra performance requires BOTH a high
ceiling (VO2max/speed) and the ability to sustain a large fraction
of that ceiling (threshold/SSmax). You can't train both at peak
intensity in the same week, so you alternate.

> "You can even use this plan without a race at the end of it!
> After 12 weeks, you should be faster and stronger to build
> toward any distance."

### Distance Ranges (Same Architecture)

| Band | Weekly character |
|------|-----------------|
| Low (intermediate) | ~30-45 mi with x-train |
| Mid (advanced) | ~50-70 mi with x-train |
| High (very advanced) | ~80-100+ mi |

### Wednesday Quality Progression (Hybrid: Speed ↔ Threshold)

| Week | Type | Structure | Effort |
|------|------|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Speed (hills) | 6×2min hills + 2mi steady | 5K + 50K |
| 2 | Speed (flat) | 16×1min/1min easy + 2mi steady | 10K→5K + 50K |
| 3 | Speed (pyramid) | 4-6×(120/60/30s) + cool-down | 10K/5K/3K |
| 4 | **Threshold (TM)** | 6-10×5min @ 1hr effort, 2min recovery | Threshold |
| 5 | Speed (threshold bridge) | 6-10×3min fast + 4×30s fast | 1hr effort→mile |
| 6 | Speed (track) | 6-10×600 + 4×200 fast | 10K→mile |
| 7 | **Threshold (TM)** | 8-12×5min @ 1hr effort, 2min recovery | Threshold |
| 8 | Speed (track) | 6-10×800 + 4×200 fast | 10K→mile |
| 9 | **Threshold (bridge)** | 4-6×5min fast + 2mi steady + PM double | 1hr effort + 50K |
| 10 | Speed (hills + tempo) | 5×3min hills + 15-20min moderate | 10K + HM |
| 11 | Threshold (taper) | 6-10×2min @ 1hr→10K + 2mi steady | 1hr→10K + 50K |
| 12 | **Final stimulus** | 3-4×5min @ 1hr + 5×30s hills → RACE | Threshold + power |

**The alternation pattern:** Weeks 1-3 are pure speed, then W4
introduces threshold on the uphill TM. After that, speed and
threshold alternate (W5 speed, W6 track, W7 threshold, W8 track,
W9 threshold). Weeks 10-12 merge both into race-specific work.

**Threshold extension is present:** W4: 6-10×5min → W7: 8-12×5min.
Same effort, growing reps. Then W9: 4-6×5min at a harder progression
(1hr effort getting harder). The threshold volume grows, the effort
specification narrows toward race intensity.

**Speed extension:** W2: 16×1min → W5: 6-10×3min → W6: 600m → W8:
800m. Both rep duration and rep distance grow across the speed weeks.

### The "Steady" Segment: Lactate Shuttling Under Fatigue

Multiple sessions end with "2 miles steady (think 50K)." This is
NOT cool-down. It's race-specific sub-threshold work performed
immediately after a hard speed session, while the body is still
processing lactate. This trains lactate shuttling — the ability to
clear lactate from working muscles and use it as fuel.

This is Canova's "extension of intensity" applied to ultra: the
athlete does hard intervals to elevate lactate, then immediately
runs at a pace that requires clearing that lactate without slowing
to recovery pace.

**Implication for generator:** For ultra race plans, Wednesday
sessions should include a post-workout "steady" segment at
race-effort pace (not easy). The `segments` schema should support
a `"post_workout_steady"` segment type with effort-based
prescription ("50K effort" or "marathon effort" depending on
target distance).

### Saturday Long Run Progression

| Week | Distance | Structured element |
|------|----------|--------------------|
| 1 | 12-16 mi | Easy/mod with vert |
| 2 | 14-18 mi | Easy/mod (volume) |
| 3 | 14-20 mi | Vert + strong downs + 5×45s hills at end (fatigue resistance) |
| 4 | 12-16 mi | 5×1mi @ 1hr effort, 1mi float (threshold long run) |
| 5 | 16-20 mi | Easy on trails with strong downs |
| 6 | 16-22 mi | Easy/mod + 5×45s hills at end (fatigue resistance) |
| 7 | 12-16 mi | "Power Hour" — 1hr mod to mod/hard (race simulation) |
| 8 | 20-25 mi | Peak long run — easy with vert, strong downs (peak volume) |
| 9 | 10-15 mi | Mod to mod/hard continuous (full race simulation) |
| 10 | 16-20 mi | Easy/mod + 4×1min hills at end (fatigue resistance) |
| 11 | 12-16 mi | Easy Z2 (taper) |
| 12 | RACE | 50K or 50 miles |

**Three distinct long run types rotate:**
1. **Easy/mod with vert** (W1,2,5,8,10) — aerobic volume + terrain
2. **Structured threshold** (W4,7,9) — race-pace or harder within
   the long run
3. **Fatigue resistance** (W3,6,10) — hills at the END of the long
   run, training neuromuscular output under glycogen depletion

**The "Power Hour" (W7):** 1 hour of moderate to mod/hard running
within a 12-16 mile long run. "View it as a race simulation with
mindset, focus, and intention." This is the ultra equivalent of the
marathon-specific long run — sustained race-relevant effort within
a volume context.

**Peak long run (W8): 20-25 miles.** Easy with vert and strong
downhills. "Go a bit higher carb than race day, emphasizing fatigue
resistance." The longest run is NOT the hardest — it's controlled,
with the hard effort shifted to W9's race simulation.

### The Race Simulation (W9 Saturday)

> "10-15 miles moderate to mod/hard... This is a race-style
> simulation, doing everything like you will do on race day (ideally
> on race-specific terrain)! This effort will stimulate the nervous
> system to be ready for the lower level effort of race day."

> "Don't overdo it — no need to go fully to the well. But this
> session will show you everything you need to know for race day!"

Three weeks before race day. Long enough to fully recover. Hard
enough to provide race-specific nervous system priming. Followed
by easy x-train the next day (not a run).

### Uphill Treadmill as a Standalone Training Day

Friday in this plan is frequently a 75-105 min Z2 uphill treadmill
session at 10-15% grade. This is NOT threshold — it's pure aerobic
development with minimal impact.

> "Z2 uphill TM improves aerobic output at LT1 without low impact
> on the body."

This is a third training modality alongside running and x-train:
uphill treadmill as a low-impact aerobic accumulation tool. The
generator should support this as a workout type distinct from both
"easy run" and "cross-training."

### Downhill Emphasis (Eccentric Contraction Training)

Repeated throughout the plan:

> "Downhills are free speed, and we want to work on eccentric
> contractions on these runs even for races without a ton of vert."

> "The goal here is to get a final big eccentric contraction
> stimulus to have the body ready to absorb muscular demands of
> race day!"

Ultra performance depends on muscular resilience under prolonged
eccentric loading. The plan deliberately prescribes "strong downs"
on long runs — running downhills faster than you need to — to
build eccentric contraction tolerance and prevent race-day
rhabdomyolysis.

**Implication for generator:** Ultra race plans should include
terrain guidance on long runs: "include downhills, run them with
purpose." For athletes with access to hills, the generator should
prefer hilly long run routes. Workout descriptions should include
the reasoning: "Strong downhills build the eccentric strength
you'll need on race day."

### Fatigue Resistance Hills

A signature element: 4-5×30-45s hills placed at the END of long
runs (W3, W6, W10). Not at the beginning — at the end.

The physiological intent: after 14-22 miles of easy/mod running,
glycogen stores are partially depleted and muscles are fatigued.
Running hard hills in this state trains the neuromuscular system
to produce power under fatigue — the exact demand of the final
third of an ultra.

**Implication for generator:** The `segments` schema should support
"end-of-run" hills as a fatigue resistance workout structure:
long easy run → short hard hills at the end. This is different
from a standard hill workout (which starts after a warm-up) and
different from a tempo run (sustained pace). It's its own category.

### Strength Training Periodization

Two routines alternate:
- **Mountain Legs:** Light, frequency-based (post-run, 2-3x/week)
- **Full Strength:** Heavier, less frequent (1-2x/week)

| Week | Wed | Sat | Sun |
|------|-----|-----|-----|
| 1-3 | Mountain Legs | Mountain Legs | Full Strength |
| 4-6 | Full Strength | Mountain Legs | Full Strength |
| 7-8 | Full/Mountain | Mountain Legs | Mountain Legs only |
| 9-11 | Mountain only | Mountain only | Mountain only |
| 12 | — | — | — |

The strength volume drops as running specificity increases. By
week 9, only Mountain Legs remains. By race week, nothing.

**Implication for generator:** Strength recommendations should be
periodized with the plan. Heavy strength in early weeks, light
maintenance in middle weeks, nothing in taper. The generator can
include strength as an annotation on workout days rather than
separate workouts.

### Structured Double Workouts (Advanced)

Two specific double workouts appear:

| Week | PM session | Structure |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| W5 | Uphill TM or x-train | 30-45 min with 5-8×2min @ 10K effort, 1min easy |
| W9 | Uphill TM or x-train | 30-45 min with 10-15×1min @ 10K effort, 30s easy |

> "Double workouts are a major adaptation signal, but are not
> necessary for peak performance, so listen to your body and brain."

These are NOT easy doubles. They are structured threshold/speed
sessions. The generator should only offer these for athletes with
established double-running history, and mark them with explicit
health/energy prerequisites.

### Heat Training Integration

Heat training appears throughout as a deliberate adaptation tool:

- **Rest days:** Sauna or hot tub 20-30 min for passive blood
  volume expansion
- **Doubles:** Heat suit option ("overdress to get a heat stimulus,
  particularly for hot races")
- **Weight vest hiking:** For race-specific hiking adaptation

> "You can do passive heat like sauna or hot tub any day that you
> have time since it doesn't add much stress."

**Implication for generator:** For athletes racing in hot
conditions, the generator should recommend passive heat on rest
days and heat-adapted doubles. Workout descriptions: "Rest day.
If possible, 20-30 min sauna or hot tub for blood volume
adaptation."

### Weight Vest Hiking Doubles

> "Start thinking weight vest hiking doubles if your race is
> hiking-heavy"

For ultra races with significant elevation gain, the generator
should offer weight-vest hiking doubles as a race-specific training
option. These replace easy jog doubles when the race involves
sustained hiking.

### Implications for StrideIQ Race Mode (Ultra)

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 12-week duration | Ultra plans default to 12 weeks. |
| Hybrid progression | Speed and threshold weeks alternate, then converge. |
| Post-workout steady | "2mi steady at 50K effort" after speed sessions for lactate shuttling. |
| Three long run types | Easy/mod with vert, structured threshold, fatigue resistance hills. |
| "Power Hour" | Race simulation as 1hr mod/hard within a long run (3 weeks out). |
| Fatigue resistance | Hills at END of long runs to train power under depletion. |
| Uphill TM as training day | 75-105 min Z2 at 10-15% grade as standalone aerobic session. |
| Downhill emphasis | "Strong downs" on long runs for eccentric contraction training. |
| Strength periodization | Heavy→light→none across the 12 weeks. |
| Structured doubles | Threshold/speed PM sessions for advanced athletes only. |
| Heat training | Passive heat on rest days, heat suit on doubles for hot races. |
| Race simulation | Full race-effort 10-15mi session 3 weeks before race day. |

## The Lower Volume Plan: The On-Ramp

The SWAP Lower Volume Plan is the feeder system. Every other plan
in the SWAP library assumes the athlete can run 20-30+ miles per
week. This 8-week plan takes someone running 12-16 mi/wk and builds
them to 20-33 mi/wk — then hands them off to any other plan
"starting at the low end of the mileage ranges."

This plan answers the question StrideIQ's generator MUST handle:
**what do you prescribe for someone who has never followed a
structured training plan?**

### Architecture

**8 weeks. 4 runs per week (with an optional 5th starting week 4).
Hike/x-train fills remaining days.**

| Week | Runs/wk | Approx weekly miles | Character |
|------|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| 1 | 4 | 12-16 | Introduction |
| 2 | 4 | 12-16 | First flat speed |
| 3 | 4 | 14-18 | Speed volume up |
| 4 | 4-5 | 18-24 | Volume push (intensity down) |
| 5 | 4-5 | 20-26 | Bring it together |
| 6 | 4-5 | 22-30 | Quality long run |
| 7 | 4-5 | 24-32 | Check-in mile |
| 8 | 4-5 | 24-33 | Peak volume, reflect |

The plan starts at 4 runs per week — NOT 5 or 6. The remaining
days are hikes and cross-training. Running volume grows by
adding distance to existing runs, not by adding runs. The 5th run
day appears optionally in week 4.

### Weekly Structure

| Day | Role |
|-----|------|
| Monday | Rest (active lifestyle, no leg pounding) |
| Tuesday | Short easy run (2-4 mi) + hill strides |
| Wednesday | KEY SESSION (hills → flat intervals → moderate runs) |
| Thursday | Hike or x-train (60-90 min). Non-impact, leg-strength focused. |
| Friday | Short easy run (2-4 mi). "Keep these runs very chill." |
| Saturday | Long run (progressive: 4-6 → 10-12 mi). The primary volume builder. |
| Sunday | Hike or x-train (1-2+ hours). Optional 5th run from W4. |

**Thursday and Sunday are NOT rest days — they are prescribed
non-running training days.** Hiking, biking, elliptical, yoga,
pilates. The pattern matches the base building plan (Thursday
x-train) but the activities are less structured and more
exploratory. "Whatever terrain makes you happiest."

### Speed Introduction (Careful, Progressive)

| Week | Wednesday session |
|------|------------------|
| 1 | 6×30s hills faster, jog down |
| 2 | 4×1min fast/2min easy + 4×30s fast/2min easy |
| 3 | 8×1min fast/90s easy |
| 4 | 4-6 mi moderate (no intervals — volume week) |
| 5 | 2×2min + 3×1min + 4×30s (descending pyramid) |
| 6 | 6×1min hills mod/hard |
| 7 | 1 mile harder + 4×30s fast (check-in mile) |
| 8 | 6-8 mi moderate (no intervals — volume week) |

**The pattern:** hills first (W1), then flat speed (W2-3), then
volume consolidation (W4), then combined (W5-6), then race
simulation (W7), then final volume (W8). Speed and volume weeks
alternate — never both at maximum in the same week.

**Effort language is even gentler than other plans:** "think smooth
and quick," "kid at recess rather than adult putting in work,"
"relaxed intensity." The beginner doesn't know what "5K effort"
feels like yet, so the plan uses sensory descriptions instead.

### Saturday Long Run Progression

| Week | Distance | Character |
|------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | 4-6 mi | Easy/mod, "hike as much as you need" |
| 2 | 4-6 mi | Moderate — "practice pushing just a bit" |
| 3 | 6-8 mi | Easy — "keeping this one very relaxed" |
| 4 | 6-8 mi | Moderate |
| 5 | 8-10 mi | Easy — "just having fun with the distance" |
| 6 | 8-10 mi | Moderate — "keeping familiarity with pushing" |
| 7 | 10-12 mi | Easy — "take this one very easy" |
| 8 | 8-10 mi | Moderate — "reflect on your growth" |

**The alternation is deliberate:** easy long run one week, moderate
the next. The beginner never faces both a longer distance AND a
harder effort simultaneously. When distance steps up, effort
steps down. When effort steps up, distance holds or drops.

**"Hike as much as you need to for the mileage!"** Run/hike is
explicitly part of the long run prescription for beginners, not
a fallback. The goal is time on feet and distance covered, not
continuous running.

### Strength: Single-Leg Step-Ups + Core Snack

No gym routine. Two exercises:

1. **Single-leg step-ups:** 30-50 reps per leg, post-run. Appears
   after every quality session and every long run. Progresses from
   30 reps (W1) to 50 reps (W4+).

2. **Core Snack routine:** 1-2x per day. Introduced W1 as the only
   strength element, maintained throughout.

> "Something like yoga, pilates, or similar activities are amazing
> throughout the plan!"

This is strength for beginners: bodyweight, post-run, progressive
but minimal. No barbells, no gym membership required.

**Implication for generator:** For athletes under 30 mi/wk, strength
recommendations should be bodyweight only. Step-ups and core. Don't
prescribe Mountain Legs or Full Strength to someone running 15 mi/wk.

### The Voice (Beginner-Specific)

The tone is distinctly different from the other plans — warmer,
more encouraging, more explicit about what's normal:

- "Keep it FUN — listen to silly playlists or podcasts, just viewing
  this as time for yourself to play."
- "Remember: no pressure on pace!"
- "Think 'kid at recess' rather than 'adult putting in work'"
- "You can always add some hiking to do more mileage as well!"
- "Reflect on your growth from the first week of the plan!"
- "WE ARE SO PROUD OF YOU!!!!!"

The coach model should detect athlete experience level and adjust
tone. A 15 mi/wk athlete gets "kid at recess." A 70 mi/wk athlete
gets "push the threshold miles, keep the floats honest."

### The Hand-Off

> "After this plan, you should be confident to build into any of
> our other plans, starting at the low end of the mileage ranges"

This is the critical design principle: the on-ramp plan's EXIT
state is the other plans' ENTRY state. The generator must ensure
that an athlete completing the lower volume plan has the base
(20-33 mi/wk, 4-5 runs/wk, hill stride experience, some speed
work) to enter the base building plan, 5K/10K plan, or beginner
ultra plan at the low end of ranges.

### Implications for StrideIQ

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 4 runs per week | Beginners run 4x/wk, not 5-7. Hike/x-train fills the other days. |
| Hike as training | Thursday and Sunday are prescribed hike/x-train, not optional rest. |
| Run/hike long runs | "Hike as much as you need" is part of the long run prescription. |
| Distance-effort alternation | Never increase both distance and effort in the same week. |
| Sensory effort cues | "Smooth and quick," "kid at recess" instead of "5K effort." |
| Bodyweight strength only | Step-ups + core. No gym routines for <30 mi/wk athletes. |
| 5th run day unlock | Optional from W4. Never forced. |
| Exit → entry alignment | Plan completion state = low-end entry for all other plans. |
| "WE ARE SO PROUD" | Coach model celebrates completion milestones for beginners. |

## The 50K Intermediate/Advanced Plan: The Missing Middle

The SWAP 50K Intermediate/Advanced plan fills the gap between the
beginner ultra (run/hike, time-based) and the champion ultra
(threshold conversion, track work). This is the plan for someone
who can run consistently but hasn't raced an ultra — or has raced
one and wants to RACE it, not just survive.

12 weeks, 31-94 miles per week range, with the same distance-range
architecture. What makes it distinct from both the beginner and
champion plans is its progression narrative: **build speed and
power first, then extend that into steady-state endurance, then
race-simulate.**

### Architecture

**12 weeks. Three-phase progression:**

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|
| **Speed + power** | 1-3 | Hill intervals (2min, 45s), flat VO2 work (1min), flat strides |
| **Threshold + steady** | 4-6 | Moderate steady runs, threshold tempos (20→30min), alternating-km long runs |
| **Race-specific** | 7-12 | Longer intervals (3×8min, 10×1min sets), race simulation (13.1mi hard), peak back-to-back, extended tempo, taper |

This is the cleanest three-phase structure in the SWAP library.
Weeks 1-3 are pure speed. Weeks 4-6 bridge speed into endurance.
Weeks 7-12 convert everything into race fitness.

### Wednesday Quality Progression

| Week | Structure | Effort | Phase |
|------|-----------|--------|-------|
| 1 | 6×2min hills + 5×45s hills | 5K | Speed |
| 2 | 15×1min/1min easy + 5×30s hills | 5K | Speed |
| 3 | 6×2min/1min + 8×1min/1min | 10K + 5K | Speed |
| 4 | 8-12 mi easy/mod steady progression | Marathon/50K | Threshold bridge |
| 5 | 1/2/3/4/3/2/1min pyramid + 5×1min | 10K + 5K | Speed+threshold |
| 6 | 5×5min + 5×30s hills | 10K (progress) | Big threshold |
| 7 | 3×8min + 6×1min hills | 10K + 5K | Long intervals |
| 8 | 20min mod/hard + 6×1min/30s | 1hr + 5K | Threshold/speed combo |
| 9 | 15min mod/hard + 4×3min | 1hr effort | Major threshold |
| 10 | 2×(10×1min/1min) | 10K | Final speed |
| 11 | 30min moderate + 4×30s hills | HM effort | Taper stimulus |
| 12 | 20min @ marathon effort | Marathon | Race week |

**Progression pattern:** Speed work dominates weeks 1-3 (short
hills, 1-2min intervals). Week 4 introduces the "steady" run —
no intervals, just sustained moderate effort. Weeks 5-6 bridge
speed and threshold. Weeks 7-9 extend interval duration (2min →
3min → 5min → 8min) while introducing threshold combos. Weeks
10-12 taper with decreasing volume but sharp stimuli.

**Key innovation — the "steady" run (W4, W5):** "Starting easy
and settling in at a steady effort progressing to marathon/50K."
This is NOT a threshold session. It's sub-threshold sustained
running — teaching the body to hold a metabolically comfortable
pace for extended periods. This is the SSmax development work
that bridges pure speed to race fitness.

### Saturday Long Run Progression

| Week | Distance | Structured element |
|------|----------|--------------------|
| 1 | 8-14 mi | Easy/mod on feel |
| 2 | 10-16 mi | 20min mod/hard @ threshold |
| 3 | 10-16 mi | Fartlek: at 20min, 1min @ threshold every 5min |
| 4 | 12-18 mi | 30min mod/hard @ threshold |
| 5 | 10-16 mi | Steady at 50K effort |
| 6 | 14-20 mi | 12mi alternating 1mi @ 1hr effort / 1mi float |
| 7 | 16-24 mi | Easy on trails (aerobic weekend) |
| 8 | 13.1 mi | RACE SIMULATION (hard on race-like trails) |
| 9 | 20-26.2 mi | Easy/mod with strong uphills @ 50K effort (peak LR) |
| 10 | 16-22 mi | 1hr moderate @ marathon effort, faster to finish |
| 11 | 12-16 mi | Easy to easy/mod (taper) |
| 12 | 50K RACE | Race day |

**Three distinct innovations here:**

1. **The race simulation (W8):** 13.1 miles hard on trails like
   the race. Not at the end of the plan — at week 8, with 4 weeks
   to absorb and build on it. "You can move this effort to any week
   between weeks 5 and 10!" The generator should place race sims
   ~4 weeks out, not ~2 weeks out.

2. **The alternating-km long run (W6):** 12 miles alternating 1mi
   @ threshold / 1mi float recovery. This is the Canova-style
   alternating workout applied to the long run — extending threshold
   stress over a much larger portion of the run than a simple tempo
   block. The generator should support alternating segments within
   long runs.

3. **The peak back-to-back (W9):** Saturday 20-26.2mi + Sunday
   14-20mi. The biggest weekend of the plan. "The big back-to-back
   will make the 50k feel short, and help you truly race it to
   potential." This is musculoskeletal resilience training — running
   long on tired legs from the day before.

### Sunday Back-to-Back Training

Back-to-back long weekends appear throughout:

| Week | Saturday | Sunday | Total weekend |
|------|----------|--------|--------------|
| 2 | 10-16 mi (w/ tempo) | 8-14 mi easy | 18-30 mi |
| 7 | 16-24 mi easy | 12-16 mi easy | 28-40 mi |
| 9 | 20-26.2 mi | 14-20 mi easy + hills | 34-46.2 mi |
| 10 | 16-22 mi (w/ tempo) | 10-16 mi easy + downs | 26-38 mi |

> "First back-to-back, great for resilience!" (W2)

**Implication for generator:** Ultra race plans should include
back-to-back long weekends. The generator should schedule these
in the second half of the plan, with the peak back-to-back ~3
weeks before race day. Sunday back-to-back runs should be easy
with strong downhills for eccentric adaptation.

### The "Steady" Run as a Distinct Workout Type

Weeks 4 and 5 introduce the steady run as a standalone session:

> "Starting easy and settling in at a steady effort progressing
> to marathon/50k. This steady running may feel a bit unnatural
> at first as the body adjusts to the new strain."

> "This should start to feel a bit more natural now." (W5)

This is the SSmax training zone in practice: a pace that sits
between easy and threshold, sustainable for very long periods
with proper fueling. For ultra runners, THIS is race pace — not
threshold, not easy, but "steady."

**Implication for generator:** The generator needs a workout type
between "easy" and "threshold" — call it "steady" or "moderate."
Prescribed at roughly marathon-to-50K effort, no intervals, just
sustained sub-threshold running. The segments schema already has
`"steady"` as a type; the generator should use it for these runs.

### Downhill Emphasis (Again)

Strong downhills appear in weeks 3, 6, 8, 10:

> "Downhills are a bit tougher on the body and require adaptation"
> (W3)

> "Running well on downhills after a tough long run is especially
> important for races with more climbing" (W6)

> "Practicing owning the downhills will be free speed on race day"
> (W10)

The generator should prescribe Sunday easy runs with explicit
downhill emphasis for ultra athletes. Workout description: "Easy
with strong, purposeful downhills. This builds the eccentric
strength for race day."

### The "Treadhill" (Uphill Treadmill, Concise)

The plan consistently offers "treadhill" as a double option:
"20-30 min at 15% grade hiking and/or running." This is the same
uphill TM concept from the champion plan but more accessible —
shorter duration, explicitly framed as a double option rather than
a standalone session.

## What NOT to Do

- Do NOT make doubles mandatory. They are always optional. The generator
  should mark them explicitly as optional.
- Do NOT prescribe doubles for athletes under ~40 km/week. The
  additional stress is not justified at lower volumes.
- Do NOT let the coach model sound certain about correlations that are
  based on limited data. Honest uncertainty IS coaching quality.
- Do NOT assume trail/ultra training is just "longer road training."
  The musculoskeletal resilience demand is qualitatively different.
- Do NOT make the coach model sound like a textbook. Roche's voice —
  human, funny, uncertain, caring — is the target. "Your threshold
  session went well. Your legs might feel heavy tomorrow. That's
  normal. Easy shake-out if you want one, or take the day off. Both
  are fine."
