# Davis: Building a Full-Spectrum 10K Plan from Scratch — Reference Note

**Source:** John Davis, Running Writings (October 2024)
**URL:** https://runningwritings.com/2024/10/percentage-based-10k-training.html
**Filed:** April 10, 2026
**Relevance:** Demonstrates Davis's from-scratch plan construction
methodology for a non-marathon distance. Directly applicable to the
generator's 10K mode and reveals how Davis thinks about the planning
process itself — which is what we need to encode.

---

## The Planning Process (how Davis builds a plan)

This is the most valuable part — not the specific workouts, but the
ORDER in which Davis builds a schedule:

1. Start with the GOAL (the race itself)
2. Work BACKWARDS from race day, placing key race-specific workouts
3. Then add specific endurance (95% race pace) and supportive
   endurance (90% race pace)
4. Then add specific speed (105% race pace) and supportive speed
   (110% race pace)
5. THEN fill in easy mileage and general endurance
6. Continually adapt and revise

**"Mileage is a means to an end, so it only makes sense to think
about mileage after we've gotten the key workouts squared away."**

This is the opposite of how most amateur plans are built (start with
mileage, then add workouts). Davis starts with the most specific work
and works outward. The generator should follow this same logic.

## The 10K Percentage Ladder

Davis's percentage system relative to 10K pace (not MP):

| % of 10K pace | Role | Example workout |
|---------------|------|-----------------|
| 45-55% | Very easy | Recovery |
| 55-65% | Easy | Daily runs |
| 70-80% | Moderate | Progression runs |
| 85-90% | Strong/supportive endurance | Long fast runs |
| 90% | Supportive endurance | 10-12 mi continuous |
| 95% | Specific endurance | 7-8 mi continuous or alternating km |
| 96-97% | Classical threshold (Daniels T) | Cruise intervals |
| 100% | Race-specific | 5×2K, 8×1K |
| 105% | Specific speed (~5K pace) | 6×1K |
| 110% | Supportive speed (~3K pace) | Sets of 500-600m |
| 115% | General speed (~mile pace) | 200-300m reps |

**Key insight:** For the 10K, paces are expressed as % of 10K pace,
not % of marathon pace. The generator should adapt the percentage
anchor based on the goal distance.

## Phase Structure for 10K (16 weeks)

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|
| General | 6 | Full-spectrum speeds, mileage building |
| Race-supportive | 4 | 90-95% and 105-110% emphasis |
| Race-specific | 6 | 95-105% dominant |

**Scaling rules:**
- 20 weeks → 8 general / 6 supportive / 6 specific
- 16 weeks → 6/4/6
- 12 weeks → 4/3/5 (specific phase gets priority)
- Less experienced → shorter specific phase (4 weeks), longer general

## 10K-Specific Workout Progression (extension-based)

| Session | Workout | Weeks before race |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | 8×1000m at 100% 10K w/ 2min jog | ~12 |
| 2 | 4 sets (1200m@100%, 800m@103-105%) w/ 4min | ~10 |
| 3 | 4×2K at 100% 10K w/ 3min jog | ~8 |
| 4 (peak) | 5×2K at 100% 10K w/ 3min jog | ~4 |
| 5 (final) | 3-2-2-1K at 100/101/102/105-107% w/ 3min | ~2 |

**This IS extension-based progression:** Same pace, longer reps,
then a mixed-pace sharpening session. Session 3→4 is pure extension
(4×2K → 5×2K). Session 1→3 is also extension (8×1K → 4×2K — same
total volume, longer individual reps).

## Float Recovery Innovation

Davis bridges from traditional threshold intervals to alternating-km
sessions via float recovery progression:

| Session | Workout |
|---------|---------|
| 1 | 6×1200m at 96-97% 10K w/ 1min jog rest |
| 2 | 6×1200m at 96-97% w/ 400m float at 80% 10K |
| 3 | 6×1200m at 96-97% w/ 400m float at 85% 10K |
| 4 | 6-7 sets (1K at 95% 10K, 1K at 80-85% 10K) |

**"The real key to improving with float recovery workouts is
increasing the speed of the float recovery over time."**

This is a progression where the RECOVERY gets harder, not the work
interval. Different from pure extension but serves the same principle:
same stress, more sustained output.

## Advanced Workout Types (for the generator's reference)

### Double Threshold (Davis's take)
- Sub-threshold: 4-5×2000m at 91-93% 10K pace, 1-2 min walk
- Supra-threshold: 16×400m at ~100% 10K pace, 20-30s walk
- Davis: "I'm still on the fence about these... Kenyan-style
  progression runs are a safe bet if you don't want to do double
  threshold."

### Evening Speed Sessions
- AM: sub-threshold endurance
- PM: short speed session (600-400-300-200m at 108-115% 10K)
- "These are the most experimental sessions... buyer beware!"

### Special Blocks (Canova-style)
- AM: 4mi at 85-90%, then 4K at 102-103%
- PM: 4mi at 85-90%, then speed sets at 110-115%
- Reserved for advanced athletes, requires significant recovery

### Kenyan-style Progression Runs
- Start extremely easy, much slower than normal
- Gradually work down to HM effort by the end
- "You could do a lot worse than just replacing a lot of the more
  advanced workouts with progression runs"

## The 5% Rule for Mixed-Speed Workouts

**"My general rule is that I only mix adjacent rungs on the ladder
of support — mixed speed workouts should only span a range of about
5% of race pace."**

This prevents galaxy-brain workouts that try to do everything and
accomplish nothing.

## Adaptation and Revision

**"The difficult part about coaching isn't writing the schedule — it's
knowing how to adapt it."** (Jamie Kirkpatrick, Davis's coaching mentor)

- Expect athletes to complete ~80% of prescribed workouts
- Rebuild the plan from scratch multiple times per cycle when needed
- The schedule must "meet in the middle" between where the athlete IS
  and where you need them to be
- Bridge workouts connect current fitness to target race-specific work

## Implications for Generator

1. **Plan construction order matters:** Start from race-specific work,
   work backwards to general. Don't start with mileage.
2. **Percentage anchor varies by distance:** 10K plans use % of 10K
   pace, not % of MP. The ladder is centered on the goal distance.
3. **Float recovery progression** is a distinct progression type the
   generator should support — recovery pace increases, not work pace.
4. **The 5% rule** constrains mixed-speed workouts — prevents the
   generator from creating unfocused sessions.
5. **Adaptation is the norm, not the exception.** "I never prescribe
   exactly the same training schedule to different people."
6. **Progression runs** are a powerful general-purpose workout that
   can substitute for many advanced sessions. The generator should
   use them liberally for developing athletes.
