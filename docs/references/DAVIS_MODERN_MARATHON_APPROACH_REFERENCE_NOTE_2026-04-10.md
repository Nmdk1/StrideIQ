# Davis Modern Marathon Approach — Reference Note

**Source:** John J. Davis, Ph.D. — "The Modern Approach to Marathon Training" (Scottish Athletics webinar + 13-week London plan)
**Filed:** April 10, 2026
**Relevance:** Race mode plan generator architecture, percentage-based pacing system, full-spectrum workout distribution, phase periodization, marathon-specific workout structures

---

## Core Framework

Davis presents an "amateurized" version of the Italian School (Canova,
Gigliotti, Rondelli, Lenzi) built on three pillars:

1. **Percentage-based pacing** — all speeds expressed as % of marathon pace
2. **Three-phase periodization** — General → Supportive → Specific
3. **Full-spectrum training** — shift emphasis, don't replace workouts

> "Training is to ADD, not to REPLACE" — Renato Canova

---

## The Ladder of Support

All paces are expressed as percentages of marathon pace (% MP). The
percentages refer to pace, not speed — so 90% MP means 10% SLOWER
than marathon pace.

| % MP | Approximate equivalent | SSmax relationship |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|
| >=120% | 1500m pace | Well above SSmax |
| 115% | ~3K pace, optimistic 5K | Above SSmax |
| 110% | 8K/10K pace | Supra-SSmax |
| 105% | Half-marathon pace, traditional threshold | SSmax boundary |
| 100% | Marathon pace | Sub-SSmax |
| 95% | **Important pace many people don't use** | Below SSmax |
| 90% | **Important pace many people don't use** | Below SSmax |
| 85% | Moderate/easy | Well below SSmax |
| 80% | Easy | Well below SSmax |
| <=75% | Very easy / recovery | Well below SSmax |

**Key insight:** 90-95% of MP doesn't correspond to any commonly raced
distance, so most runners never train there. This is the gap that the
modern approach fills — "long and fast, but not quite marathon pace."

## Three Phases of Periodization

### General Phase

Building raw materials. Running long. Running fast. Wide range of speeds.
But NOT putting them together yet. This is "training for training."

- Paces: broad spectrum, emphasizing the outer rungs (115-120%, 75-85%)
- Workouts: progression runs, hill fartlek, track repeats, long easy runs
- Fitness estimation: use % of 5K pace (not MP) because MP may be unknown
  or stale

**Davis's key insight for general phase:** Use 5K pace as the anchor, not
marathon pace. Everyone knows their 5K (or can do a parkrun this weekend).
You can't do that with a marathon. This maps directly to StrideIQ's RPI
system — RPI gives 5K equivalent pace as the primary anchor.

### Marathon-Supportive Phase

Starting to combine long + fast, but not yet at marathon-specific speeds.

- Emphasis: 110% MP (speed support) and 90% MP (endurance support)
- Key workouts: Long repeats at 110% MP (4×2K), long fast runs at 90% MP
- Volume: stabilize at or near peak

### Marathon-Specific Phase

Building the exact race you want. Long and fast at marathon pace.

- Emphasis: 95-105% MP
- Key workouts: alternating km sessions, stepwise long runs, marathon pace runs
- Volume: LESS important — emphasis on big stress + big recovery
- Mileage becomes a RANGE, not a target

## Key Workout Structures

### Stepwise Long Fast Run (90-96% MP)

The signature marathon endurance workout. Stepwise descending pace:

```
10K at 90% → 8K at 92% → 8K at 94% → 6K at 96% MP
(32K total)
```

Progression across weeks:
- Week 6: 10-8-7K at 90, 92, 94% (25K)
- Week 8: 30-32K at 90-92% (30K)
- Week 10: 10-8-8-6K at 90-92-94-96% (32K)

### Alternating Kilometer Marathon Pace (100% MP)

Continuous running with structured float recovery:

```
6 × (3K at 100% MP, 1K at 85% MP)
(24K total, 18K at MP)
```

Progression:
- Week 6: 12-14K continuous at 100% MP
- Week 7: 16-18K continuous at 100% MP
- Week 9: 8 × (2K at 100%, 1K at 85%) — 24K total
- Week 11: 6 × (3K at 100%, 1K at 85%) — 24K total, more extension

The float recovery is NOT easy — it's 85% MP, still moving well.
Advanced runners can bring this up to 90% MP.

### Alternating Kilometer Speed (105% MP)

Half-marathon pace with float recovery:

```
6-7 × (1K at 103-105% MP, 1K at 90% MP)
```

Progression:
- Week 6: 5-6 × (1200m at 105%, 400m at 90%)
- Week 7: 8-10K continuous at 105%
- Week 9: 7-8 × (1K at 105%, 1K at 85%)
- Week 11: 6-8K at 105% (regenerative)
- Week 12: 6-7 × (1K at 103-105%, 1K at 90%)

**Key progression insight:** Recovery pace improves over the cycle. This
is the best indicator of marathon-specific fitness at this intensity —
being able to float the recovery fast, not hitting the fast K faster.

### Long Repeats for Speed Support (110% MP)

Marathon-supportive speed work:

```
4 × 2K at 108-110% MP with 4 min jog
```

Progression: 8×1K → 5×1600m → 4×2K (same total volume, more extension)

**Endurance-oriented runner adjustment:** 110% MP may be too fast. Try
108%. If HM pace is closer to 103% than 105%, this runner is
endurance-oriented and should back off the speed numbers while going
longer on the endurance workouts.

## Regenerative Workouts

Between big sessions, include "regenerative" workouts — touching on a
fitness quality without pushing hard. Difficulty 7/10, not 9/10.

```
Sat: 10-8-8-6K stepwise (9/10) — big workout
Sun: Rest
Mon: 6-10K very easy
Tue: 10-14K easy
Wed: 6-8K at 105% MP (7/10) — regenerative
Thu: 12-14K easy
Fri: 10-12K easy
Sat: 6×(3K at 100%, 1K at 85%) (8.5-9/10) — big workout
```

This is why mileage doesn't matter in the specific phase — the pattern
is big stress → big recovery → regenerate → big stress.

## Implications for StrideIQ Plan Generator

### 1. The percentage-based system maps directly to RPI

StrideIQ's RPI calculator provides the 5K pace anchor. For general phase
workouts, use % of 5K pace (as Davis recommends). For supportive and
specific phases, convert to % of predicted marathon pace.

The plan generator should express paces BOTH as absolute times AND as
% MP so the athlete understands the relationship between workouts.

### 2. Race mode needs three-phase periodization, not four

Current n1_engine uses base → build → peak → taper. Davis's model is
general → supportive → specific (with taper folded into specific). The
Canova-influenced approach treats the specific phase as containing both
peak and taper — the biggest workouts happen 2-3 weeks before race day,
then volume drops naturally.

Consider aligning the phase labels in Race mode to match this more
modern framework, or at minimum, mapping the internal phases to the
correct workout types per phase.

### 3. The alternating kilometer workout structure

This is the most distinctive aspect of the modern marathon approach.
The plan generator should support this as a workout structure:

```json
{
  "segments": [
    {"distance_km": 3.0, "pace_pct_mp": 100, "label": "marathon pace"},
    {"distance_km": 1.0, "pace_pct_mp": 85, "label": "float recovery"},
    {"distance_km": 3.0, "pace_pct_mp": 100, "label": "marathon pace"},
    {"distance_km": 1.0, "pace_pct_mp": 85, "label": "float recovery"},
    ...
  ]
}
```

This is NOT intervals with rest. The float is still running at a
meaningful pace. The plan generator's `segments` JSONB field already
supports this structure.

### 4. Mileage becomes a range in the specific phase

The plan generator currently targets exact weekly mileage. In the
specific phase, this should become a range: "72-80 km" instead of "76 km."
The athlete adjusts based on how they feel after big workouts.

### 5. Workout support across speeds AND across time

Each workout category should show a clear progression:
- Week 6: 5×1200m → Week 9: 7-8×1K/1K → Week 12: 6×1K/1K (recovery faster)
- Week 6: 25K stepwise → Week 8: 30K at 90-92% → Week 10: 32K stepwise

The plan generator should track these progressions and ensure each
workout is a reasonable step from the previous one in the same category.

### 6. General phase uses 5K anchor, not marathon pace

For athletes without a recent marathon (or whose marathon fitness is
stale), the general phase should use RPI-derived 5K pace as the workout
anchor. Convert to % MP at the transition to supportive phase when the
athlete has enough data to estimate current marathon fitness.

### 7. The endurance-oriented vs speed-oriented adjustment

Davis explicitly calls out that some runners find 105% and 110% MP too
fast. These endurance-oriented marathoners should use 103% and 108%
instead. The plan generator should detect this from the athlete's race
equivalencies (if HM pace is 103% MP instead of 105%, use lower numbers).

### 8. Regenerative workouts

Not every quality session is a "hard" session. The plan generator should
include regenerative-effort workouts (7/10 difficulty) between big
sessions to touch on fitness qualities without adding training stress.
This is the opposite of the old-school "every workout is hard" mentality.

---

## What NOT to Do

- Do NOT adopt a rigid 5% ladder. Davis himself says 103%, 108%, etc.
  are common. Use the athlete's actual equivalencies from RPI.
- Do NOT prescribe marathon pace workouts in the general phase for
  athletes who don't know their current marathon pace. Use 5K-anchored
  paces instead.
- Do NOT treat mileage as a target in the specific phase. It's a range.
  The emphasis is on quality workouts and recovery, not hitting a number.
- Do NOT schedule races in the last 4-5 weeks before the goal marathon.
  The specific phase intentionally trades HM/10K fitness for marathon
  fitness. A poor HM result 4 weeks out is expected and demotivating.
- Do NOT copy this 13-week plan verbatim. It's a worked example for one
  mileage tier. The plan generator should adapt durations, volumes, and
  pace ranges to the individual athlete's FitnessBank data.
