## The Seven Principles (extracted from interviews)

### 1. Plans Written in Pencil

**"I tell her nothing is written in pen, it's all in pencil — we
change workouts minutes before they start sometimes. Everything is
adaptation."**

This is not carelessness. It's a deliberate coaching philosophy:
the plan is a hypothesis, not a contract. The coach observes, the
athlete reports, and together they decide what today's training
should be — regardless of what was written on the schedule.

**Implication for StrideIQ:** Distance ranges, effort-based
prescription, and the replanner are the technical implementations
of "pencil plans." The athlete should always feel like they have
agency over their training.

### 2. Teach, Don't Dictate

**"My goal with coaching people is to teach them and have them
understand why we're doing what we're doing so if I ever stop
coaching them, or for whatever reason we part ways, that they are
basically able to coach themselves the same way I was coaching them
previously. My job is to eliminate my own job, I guess."**

Green's goal is to make himself unnecessary. He teaches athletes
to read their own bodies, understand the purpose of each session,
and make independent decisions.

**Implication for StrideIQ:** Workout descriptions should include
the WHY, not just the WHAT. "This threshold session builds your
ability to sustain race effort" teaches. "Run 4×5min at threshold"
just instructs.

### 3. Fun is Performance

**"Having fun is also super important and I think that improves
their performance at the end of the day."**

**"You can have fun with it, enjoy it and still do well."**

This isn't motivational fluff. Green genuinely believes that
enjoyment drives consistency, consistency drives adaptation,
and adaptation drives performance. The causal chain is:
fun → consistency → fitness → results.

**Implication for StrideIQ:** The coaching voice should convey
that running is a privilege, not a punishment. Workout descriptions
should make athletes want to do the session, not dread it.

### 4. Unremarkable Consistency Over Heroic Sessions

**"If you look at her training, we didn't do anything special. It
wasn't even a particularly good build up."**

For Seidel's Olympic Trials marathon:
- Never went faster than 10K pace in the entire build
- Everything was "longer, aerobic work"
- Only 12 miles at marathon pace (vs. 18-20 for experienced
  marathoners like Flanagan/Huddle)
- The build was "consistently unremarkable"

**"She knows in the end, that if she's consistent, she's a danger."**

**Implication for StrideIQ:** The generator should not over-prescribe
intensity. For athletes early in development, or athletes preparing
for a first marathon, less is more. The magic is in the consistency
of the easy days, not the brilliance of the hard ones.

### 5. The Athlete Knows Their Body Better Than the Coach

**"Molly should trust Molly more than anyone else in the world."**

**"She's not someone who blindly follows. That's not the athlete she
is... She wants to understand. If she wants to push it a little bit,
she doesn't have to ask my permission."**

Green doesn't monitor heart rate. He doesn't use lactate monitors.
He relies on athlete self-reporting and feel. "For now, we're going
to rely on feel."

**Implication for StrideIQ:** The system informs, the athlete
decides. Never override the athlete's judgment about their own body.
When the data says one thing and the athlete says another, the
athlete wins.

### 6. Leave the Athlete Wanting More

When Mike Smith (Galen Rupp's coach) advised Green on Seidel's
marathon prep, the key message was:

**"You want her to be excited for her next build. About what's
next to come and what mileage she can do."**

Don't exhaust every adaptation in one cycle. Leave room to grow.
The current block should set up the next block, not max everything
out.

**Implication for StrideIQ:** Build-over-build progression serves
this principle technically. But the coaching voice should also
convey it: "There's more to come. This is the beginning, not the
peak."

### 7. Document Honestly, Don't Romanticize

After Seidel's Olympic Trials success, Green had her write down
exactly how she felt through the training cycle:

**"By writing things down, you can't romanticize it when you look
back at it 12 months later — you write down the good and the bad,
and you see those negatives in black and white. That grounds you
in your next cycle."**

**Implication for StrideIQ:** The Personal Operating Manual and
activity logs serve this purpose. Show the athlete the full picture
— the bad workouts alongside the good ones. Don't filter out the
difficult days.

## Green on COVID Disruption (2020)

When the Olympics were postponed:

**"Any extra time we have is going to be a benefit to us. For now
we just have the time to enjoy running without any structure, so
just doing mileage, which is what we do best."**

**"We're taking everything in our stride. If that means we end up
doing a time trial in the fall, so be it. We might start in
Hopkinton and try and run to Boston as a time trial. That's fine.
It is what it is. You gotta roll with the punches."**

This is the adaptive philosophy in action under extreme
uncertainty. No panic, no over-correction. Just keep running,
keep enjoying it, and adapt when the situation becomes clearer.

## Green's Coaching Background

Green's approach was shaped by:
- His own experience as a Georgetown runner
- Mike Smith (coached Galen Rupp) — consulted on marathon prep
- Scott Fauble / Ben Rosario — read "Inside a Marathon"
- His own instincts: "I mixed that all together and made a plan
  that I thought would set her up best"
- Coaching Mary Cain's professional team (Atalanta NYC)

No formal coaching education. No certification. Just pattern
recognition, athlete empathy, and adaptive decision-making.

**This is important for StrideIQ's philosophy:** The best coaching
isn't about credentials or systems — it's about understanding the
individual athlete and adapting in real time. The system should
enable this kind of coaching at scale, not replace it with rigidity.
