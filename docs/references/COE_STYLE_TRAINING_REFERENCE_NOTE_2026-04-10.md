# Coe-Style Training — Reference Note

**Source:** John J. Davis, Ph.D. — "Coe-style elite 800m, 1500m, and mile training from 'Winning Running'"
**Published:** 2026
**Filed:** April 10, 2026
**Relevance:** Periodization models, multi-pace workout systems, circuit training periodization, coaching philosophy for plan generator and coach tier

---

## Core Concept

Peter Coe saw middle-distance events as a game of speed endurance —
developing 400m speed was paramount, and training focused on maintaining
speed under fatigue. He emphasized quality over volume, calling slow
distance "a necessary evil to be done in the minimum dose possible."

The modern verdict: Coe-style training is viable for the 800m, can work
for 1500m athletes with the right physiological profile (natural speed +
strong aerobic base), but its narrow intensity-first approach caused a
measurable decline in middle-distance performance when widely adopted in
the UK during the 1990s-2000s. The counter-evidence is clear: broader,
more aerobically balanced systems (Canova, Daniels, Norwegian) produce
more consistent results across more athletes.

## The "One-Man Plan" Warning

> "Beware the one-man plan. Humans are storytelling creatures, so we
> love tales of the intrepid coach who single-handedly bested the rest
> with a bold new training plan, but what you should really look for
> are systems that work for different athletes, in different events, in
> different parts of the world, and in different eras of athletics."

Sebastian Coe was the only international-caliber athlete Peter Coe ever
coached. His system was optimized for one extraordinary individual.

**Direct mapping to StrideIQ:** This is the entire reason N=1 exists.
Population-based training systems fail because they're optimized for an
average that doesn't exist. The correlation engine discovers what works
for each individual athlete. The plan generator must never prescribe a
single system — it must adapt to the individual's response patterns.

## Coe's Four Training Zones

| Zone | Coe label | Pace range | SSmax mapping | Lactate (mM) |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| 1 | Aerobic conditioning | Easy runs, 6:00-6:15/mi | Well below SSmax | 2.0-3.5 |
| 2 | Anaerobic conditioning | Threshold / sub-threshold, HM-10K pace | Sub-SSmax | 3.5-5.0 |
| 3 | Aerobic capacity | 10K to 3K pace, 800-3000m reps | SSmax to supra-SSmax | 5.0-8.0 |
| 4 | Anaerobic capacity | 1500m to 400m pace, 200-1000m reps | Well above SSmax | 8.0-9.0+ |

**Connection to SSmax reference note:** Coe's Zone 2 maps to sub-SSmax
work (metabolically sustainable quality). Zone 3 spans the SSmax boundary.
Zone 4 is firmly supra-SSmax. The SSmax framework explains WHY Coe's zone
structure works physiologically — each zone targets a different side of
the metabolic stability boundary.

## 12-Month Periodization Model

| Phase | Focus | Duration | Zones emphasized |
|-------|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | Rest | 4 weeks | None |
| 2 | Transition | 8 weeks | Zone 1, easy circuits |
| 3 | Preparation | 21 weeks | All zones, progressive intensity |
| 4 | Pre-competition | 5 weeks | Zones 3-4 dominant |
| 5 | Early competition | 8 weeks | Zone 4, racing |
| 6 | Main competition | 6 weeks | Race-specific, peaking |

**Key insight for Build/Maintain modes:** Coe's preparation phase (21
weeks) is where the real work happens. Build mode's 4-week blocks are
micro-cycles within a preparation-like macro-cycle. The rotation of
sub-threshold (Block 1) → supra-threshold (Block 2) → mixed (Block 3)
follows Coe's principle of progressively adding intensity to a base of
aerobic conditioning.

## Canova's Critique (the definitive modern take)

> "In training, the most important problem is not what athletes do, but
> what athletes DON'T DO."

> "There is not some type of training which is wrong. Wrong are the
> PERCENTAGES of different type of training, that, approaching the
> competition season, must become more specific."

**Direct mapping to StrideIQ:** The plan generator should never eliminate
a training stimulus — it should adjust the PROPORTION based on the
athlete's phase, goals, and individual response. Build mode should include
some speed work even in early blocks. Maintain mode should include some
aerobic capacity work even though it's not "building." The correlation
engine detects which stimuli produce the best individual response and
the plan generator adjusts proportions accordingly.

## Circuit Training Periodization

One of Coe's most useful and under-appreciated contributions. Directly
relevant to StrideIQ's cross-training integration.

**Structure:**
- Reps per exercise: half of max reps in 45-60 seconds
- 5-12 exercises per circuit
- 15 seconds between exercises
- 1:1 work-to-rest ratio between sets
- 2-5 sets depending on difficulty level (easy/medium/hard)

**Annual periodization of circuits:**

| Phase | Circuit level | Lifting level |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Rest | None | None |
| Transition | Easy | Light |
| Early preparation | Hard | Medium |
| Mid preparation | Moderate | Heavy |
| Late preparation | Easy | Medium |
| Pre-competition | Easy | Light |
| Competition | None | None |

**Key pattern:** Circuit intensity peaks early in preparation, then
decreases as running intensity increases. This is the inverse relationship
between strength work and race-specific work. The plan generator should
follow this pattern when prescribing cross-training alongside running.

## Multi-Pace Training (Horwill System)

Coe used Frank Horwill's five-pace system: training at 400m, 800m, 1500m,
3K, and 5K pace within a 12-14 day cycle. Each pace targets a different
energy system and adaptation.

**Example Coe training block:**

| Day | Workout |
|-----|---------|
| 1 | 4 × 1600m at 5K pace |
| 2 | Fartlek |
| 3 | 8 × 800m at 3K pace |
| 4 | Road run |
| 5 | 16 × 200m at mile pace |
| 6 | Rest or fartlek |
| 7 | Race or time trial |
| 8 | 4-6 × 400m at 800m pace |
| 9 | Road run |
| 10 | Sprint work at 400m pace |
| 11 | Fartlek |
| 12 | Time trial or any five-pace workout |

**Relevance to StrideIQ:** The multi-pace concept maps to the plan
generator's quality session variety. Even in Build mode (which has no race
target), the generator should expose the athlete to multiple paces —
not just "threshold." The SSmax reference note specifies sub-threshold
and supra-threshold as the two key quality zones, but within those zones,
varying the rep length and pace range provides broader adaptation.

## Implications for StrideIQ

### Plan Generator

1. **Build mode quality rotation should include pace variety within each
   block type.** Block 1 (sub-threshold focus) should still include some
   faster strides or short pickups. Block 2 (supra-threshold focus) should
   still include one sub-threshold session per week. "Add, not replace."

2. **The preparation phase dwarfs all others.** In Coe's model, 21 of 52
   weeks are preparation. Build mode IS preparation for athletes without
   a race — it should feel like purposeful, progressive development, not
   maintenance.

3. **Circuit/strength periodization** should inversely correlate with
   running intensity when cross-training integration is expanded. Peak
   strength work in early blocks, decreasing as quality running sessions
   increase.

### Coach Tier

4. **Coaches working with mid-distance athletes** need pace prescriptions
   across the full spectrum (400m through 5K pace), not just easy/threshold/
   intervals. The Custom week designer should support workout types at all
   these pace ranges.

5. **Race-specific work increases as competition approaches.** The plan
   generator's Race mode already handles this via periodization phases
   (base → build → peak → taper). Build mode doesn't have this
   directionality, which is correct — it's general development.

### Knowledge Base / Coach Prompt

6. **The Canova principle** — "the most important problem is not what
   athletes do, but what athletes DON'T DO" — should be in the coach's
   system prompt. When reviewing an athlete's training, the coach should
   identify what's MISSING, not just evaluate what's present. An athlete
   doing only easy miles and threshold is missing speed work. An athlete
   doing only intervals is missing aerobic development.

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT implement Coe's system as a plan template. It was designed for
  one elite male 800m/1500m runner. The plan generator must remain N=1.
- Do NOT use Coe's absolute pace recommendations (5:30/mi for easy runs).
  All paces derive from the athlete's RPI.
- Do NOT adopt Coe's dismissal of volume. The evidence shows that
  intensity-first approaches underperform balanced systems for the
  majority of athletes. The correlation engine will learn the right
  balance for each individual.
- Do NOT use Coe's lactate values — we don't measure lactate. HR and
  pace stream data provide sufficient proxies for steady-state detection
  (see SSmax reference note).
