## CATEGORY 0 — GOVERNING PRINCIPLES (Proposed)

**GP-1:** The correct question is never "what phase are we in." It is: what physiological adaptations does this athlete most need to achieve the goal, and what are their limiters? Limiter analysis drives session selection at every point in the plan. Phase structure is a scaffold for athletes without fingerprint data. When fingerprint exists, limiter analysis governs.

**GP-2:** Individual data beats book principle. When fingerprint conflicts with population default, fingerprint wins. We do not create outliers, we recognize and serve them.

**GP-3:** Hard → easy until ready. Readiness determines the next hard session. Recovery coefficient from fingerprint is the signal. Percentage targets are outcomes, not prescriptions.

## CATEGORY 2: WEEKLY STRUCTURE RULES

**WS-1** | `[RECLASSIFIED: HARD → SOFT]`
Original rule: Never 3 quality sessions in one week. Max 2 Level 4+ sessions.
Reframe: Session count cap is the wrong variable. The correct framework is a development ladder:

Stage 1 — Beginner (0-6 months, still adapting): Zero quality sessions. Build frequency, consistency, and mileage only. Add a day before adding miles. Add miles before adding intensity. Wait for adaptation signals before introducing any quality. Quality introduced too early is the primary beginner injury driver.

Stage 2 — Early intermediate (6+ months, adapted, plateau emerging): Introduce threshold first. 1 quality session per week. The 2-session cap is a reasonable guard at this stage.

Stage 3 — Intermediate to advanced (adapted, consistent, responding well): 3 quality sessions per week is the natural and correct structure — intervals, threshold, long run. This is not aggressive, it is the standard framework for a developed runner.

Stage 4 — Advanced/masters with fingerprint: Session frequency determined entirely by limiter analysis and fingerprint recovery response. No cap. Founder (57yo) runs 4 quality sessions per week in a crash block.

Evaluator: assess athlete tier and adaptation status before applying any session frequency rule.

---

**WS-2** | `[RECLASSIFIED: HARD → SOFT]`
Rule: Saturday before Sunday long run is ALWAYS easy. Never medium-long, never quality.
Founder note: "Always easy" is a beginner/intermediate protection rule. Advanced athletes with fingerprint evidence supporting back-to-back quality can and do run quality Saturday into quality long Sunday — intervals into climbing long, 800s into mountain long. Documented in founder's training history across multiple months. The rule exists to protect athletes who have not built the structural and aerobic foundation to absorb consecutive stress. The Saturday/Sunday pairing can be a deliberate pre-fatigue overload stimulus — not careless stacking. Evaluator: flag back-to-back quality as a warning for beginner/intermediate athletes only. Advanced athletes with fingerprint support: no restriction.

---

**WS-3** | `[REFRAMED]`
Original rule: MP long run weeks: no threshold work that week. Exception: 70+ mpw with explicit recovery.
Reframe: If hard session precedes the long run, the long run must be easy. If the long run contains quality (MP segments, HMP finish, race simulation), the preceding hard session must be separated by adequate easy days. The marquee session — whichever it is — gets protected. What follows or precedes it must be easy. The 70+ mpw exception language is retired — it is not about volume, it is about session sequencing and recovery between hard efforts.
Classification: `[HARD]` on the principle. `[SOFT]` on specific spacing (N=1 dependent).

---

**WS-4** | `[RETIRED — merged into WS-3]`
Original rule: Threshold weeks: long run is easy (no MP/HMP segments).
Founder note: Redundant. Same principle from the other direction. Both reduce to: hard session and long run cannot both be hard in the same window. Absorbed into WS-3.

---

**WS-5** | `[SOFT]` — no change, reframed
Rule: Structure A (quality midweek + easy long) and Structure B (easy week + MP long) must alternate in build/peak.
Founder note: Strict A/B alternation is a population scaffold for intermediate athletes who need structured recovery built in. Advanced athletes do not alternate mechanically — they sustain high quality load across multiple weeks until physical and mental fatigue signals indicate a cutback is needed, then take it. The cutback is responsive, not calendar-driven. Founder's training log confirms: sustained 60-70+ mpw quality weeks followed by responsive cutbacks to 44-51 miles when accumulated fatigue warranted it. Evaluator: do not flag consecutive high-quality weeks as a violation for advanced athletes. Flag the absence of any cutback over an extended period as a warning signal.

---

**WS-6** | `[RECLASSIFIED: HARD → SOFT]`
Rule: Level 4+ session requires at least 1 Level ≤2 day before next Level 4+.
Founder note: Rule requires correct session classification before it can be applied. Elevation and terrain do not automatically elevate a session to Level 4+. A 14-mile run with 1,500 feet of climbing at low zone 2 HR is an easy/aerobic session despite mechanical demands — level is determined by physiological intensity (HR, pace relative to threshold, RPE), not surface characteristics. For advanced athletes, back-to-back sessions that appear hard by distance or terrain may both be aerobically easy. True back-to-back Level 4+ (intervals followed by threshold) is manageable for advanced athletes with fingerprint support and is documented in founder's training history. Rule is a useful guard for beginner/intermediate athletes where any elevated session needs a recovery day.

---

**WS-7** | `[HARD]` — confirmed, floor holds
Rule: Level 5 (MP long, race) requires at least 2 Level ≤2 days before next Level 4+.
Founder note: Rule holds across all athlete tiers. MP long run and races are events — they create systemic fatigue that requires genuine recovery before the next hard stimulus. 2 easy days minimum is the floor, not the ceiling. Advanced athletes may recover faster than the population average but even for exceptional athletes an MP long run is not treated casually. N=1 recovery signals (HRV, fatigue, leg feel) determine if more time is needed beyond the 2-day floor.

---

**WS-8** | `[SOFT]` — confirmed, safety valve validated
Rule: 5 weeks of combined T + MP work is the ceiling before mandatory cutback.
Founder note: Rule stands. The 5-week ceiling is a genuine safety valve. Sustained combined T + MP work beyond 5 weeks without a cutback pushes cumulative fatigue into the injury risk zone regardless of athlete tier. Founder's own training history supports this — hip/femoral issues that led to DNS Houston emerged in a period of extended high-quality accumulation without adequate recovery. The cutback is not optional beyond 5 weeks — it is injury prevention. Advanced athletes may feel capable of continuing but structural and hormonal stress accumulates whether they feel it or not. The timing and depth of the cutback are N=1 dependent. The ceiling itself is not negotiable.

## CATEGORY 4: SESSION SIZING RULES

**SS-1** | `[HARD]` — reframed to time-based cap
Original rule: Threshold session ≤ 10% of weekly volume.
Reframe: Percentage-based cap is the wrong unit. Distance varies by athlete pace — 10% of weekly volume at 6:30 pace is very different physiological stress than 10% at 9:00 pace. Time is the correct unit. 40 minutes of threshold work in a session is a lot — it is the absolute ceiling, not a target. Most threshold sessions should be well under 40 minutes of work time. The percentage rule may accidentally under-prescribe for slow athletes and over-prescribe for fast high-volume athletes.
New rule: Threshold session capped at 40 minutes of work time maximum. Most sessions should be under 30 minutes of work. Percentage rule retired.

---

**SS-2** | `[HARD]` — reframed to total volume
Original rule: Interval session ≤ 8% of weekly volume or 10K distance, whichever is less.
Reframe: Percentage is the wrong unit. Total interval volume per session is the correct anchor — founder consistently runs 4-6 miles of interval work per session regardless of rep length or target pace. What varies is composition: 5K training uses shorter reps at 5K pace; 10K training builds to longer reps (10×1K) at 10K pace; total volume stays in the same range. Rule reframed as: total interval volume per session 4-6 miles for advanced athletes, scaled proportionally for intermediate and beginner athletes. Percentage of weekly volume serves as population default only when fingerprint data is absent.
New rule: Total interval volume per session is the correct unit. 4-6 miles per session for advanced athletes. Goal-specific composition within that volume envelope. `[HARD]` on total volume ceiling. `[SOFT]` on composition.

---

**SS-3** | `[HARD]` — reframed to total volume
Original rule: Repetition session ≤ 5% of weekly volume or 5mi, whichever is less.
Reframe: Same correction as SS-2. Percentage is the wrong unit, total volume is correct. 4 miles of repetition work per session is consistent with founder's practice (16×400 = 4 miles of work). 5-mile ceiling is reasonable as a hard cap — beyond that fatigue accumulates and rep quality degrades regardless of fitness level. Percentage serves as population default only.
New rule: Total rep volume per session. 4 miles typical for advanced athletes. 5-mile absolute ceiling — quality degrades beyond that. `[HARD]` on 5-mile ceiling.

---

**SS-4** | `[HARD]` — confirmed, additions made
Rule: MP in long run ≤ 20% of weekly volume or 18mi, whichever is less.
Founder note: Rule holds directionally. Two additions: (1) MP work accumulates across the week — a Tuesday MP medium-long plus a Sunday MP long run in the same week must be counted together against the weekly cap, not in isolation. (2) Beyond 18 miles at MP for non-professional athletes is racing, not training. The physiological and recovery cost crosses from productive stimulus into race-level depletion. Non-professionals lack the recovery infrastructure to absorb that load as training. 18mi is the hard ceiling because it is the point where the effort stops being a training stimulus.

**SS-5** | `[RECLASSIFIED: HARD → SOFT]`

Reframe: Percentage tracking is the wrong framework. The correct principle: hard session → easy until ready to go hard again. ## CATEGORY 5: THRESHOLD PROGRESSION RULES

**TP-1** | `[HARD]` on progression principle — `[SOFT]` on path
Rule: T-block shows week-over-week progression (intervals → longer intervals → continuous for marathon only).
Founder note: Continuous threshold is not the required endpoint and is not marathon-exclusive. Multiple valid progression paths exist. Rep-count progression (2×1mi → 2×1.5mi → 2×2mi) is one valid arc. Duration progression (6×5min → 7×5min → 8×5min → 30-35min continuous) is another. The principle is week-over-week progressive overload of the threshold stimulus — the specific path is athlete and goal dependent. Continuous threshold carries higher recovery cost than equivalent-volume cruise intervals and has a significant psychological component — it can build confidence or destroy it. Should be used deliberately, not as a default endpoint. 5K/10K athletes rarely need it at all.

It is neither short enough to be cruise intervals nor long enough to be meaningful continuous threshold. Low tier progression should use natural interval structures athletes recognize and can execute mentally.
Revised low tier: 4×4min → 5×5min → 6×5min or 4×5min → 2×10min → 20-25min continuous (optional).
Mid tier (5×5min to 25min continuous) and high tier (6×5min to 40min continuous) stand as written. 40-minute ceiling for high tier confirmed consistent with SS-1.

## CATEGORY 6: INTERVAL AND REPETITION RULES

**IR-1** | `[SOFT]` — confirmed, prescription guidance added
Rule: Intervals are safe in base phase (fresh legs). Risky in mid-build (high cumulative fatigue).
Founder note: The underlying principle holds — intervals on accumulated fatigue carry higher injury risk. This is not a reason to avoid them, it is a reason to be smart about them. Life carries risk. When intervals are needed mid-build or during a crash block, the prescription changes: no hero workouts, controlled effort, touch the system and move on. Reduce rep count or total volume before reducing intensity — maintain quality of each rep, not quantity. The stimulus is what drives adaptation. Evaluator: flag mid-build intervals as a caution note, not a block. Ensure session prescription reflects accumulated fatigue context.

---

**IR-2** | `[RETIRED as written]`
Original rule: Athlete at 65+ mpw with 3 weeks of MP work should NOT add interval sessions.
Replacement: At 65+ mpw the correct governors are fingerprint, goal, and athlete optionality — not a blanket prohibition. Founder's Houston build ran intervals, threshold, and MP work simultaneously at 65-70mpw across multiple weeks. The rule is trying to protect against cumulative fatigue overload at high volume, which is a real concern, but a categorical ban is the wrong mechanism. The correct check is recovery profile and fatigue accumulation signals.
New rule: At 65+ mpw, interval prescription is governed by fingerprint recovery profile, goal requirements, and athlete preference. Evaluator flags high cumulative load as a caution, not a block.

---

**IR-3** | `[REFRAMED — tier and age restrictions revised]`
Original rule: For advanced 5K: 200m and 300m REPS at 1500m pace during build. Uses repetitions stem.
Reframe: Rule is too restrictive on athlete tier. 200m and 300m reps at 1500m pace are not exclusive to advanced athletes — intermediate athletes and masters athletes of all ages benefit enormously from this work. Neuromuscular stimulus at speeds above race pace is valuable across the development spectrum. Founder's 79-year-old father broke 5 state age group records running repeats at 1500m pace — exceptional neuromuscular adaptation at an age most frameworks would exclude from this work. Age-based restrictions on this type of work are not supported by physiology or real-world results. The correct filter: can the athlete execute the rep at target pace with good form and full recovery between reps? One age-based constraint does apply: athletes 75+ should be limited to 100m and 200m repeats — not 300m. At 75+ the structural and biomechanical risk of 300m at near-maximal speed outweighs the additional stimulus.
New rule: Appropriate for intermediate athletes and above, all ages. 75+ limited to 100m-200m maximum rep length. `[SOFT]` on frequency — every 2-3 weeks default. Execution quality and full rep recovery are the primary governors.

## CATEGORY 7: RECOVERY AND SPACING RULES

Crash block exception applies for advanced athletes with fingerprint support — documented under PH-1. Outside of deliberate crash block protocol, even advanced athletes benefit from at least 1 easy day between threshold and the next hard session.

**RC-2** | `[RECLASSIFIED: HARD → SOFT]`
Rule: After intervals: 1-2 easy days before next Level 4+ (N=1; more for masters/high fatigue).
Founder note: "1-2 easy days" is too rigid and misses individual recovery curves. Founder's pattern: performance is often enhanced the day after intervals — the acute adaptation and neuromuscular activation produces a performance rebound. DOMS and real fatigue arrive on day 2, not day 1. This means threshold or long runs the day after intervals can be productive and even optimal for this athlete. Recovery curve shape is N=1 — some athletes peak the next day, others need 48 hours. The recovery coefficient in StrideIQ's fingerprint system should capture this individual curve and use it to time subsequent hard sessions.
